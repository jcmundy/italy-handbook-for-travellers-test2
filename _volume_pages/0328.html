{% raw %}level. In the 10th cent, the arch was converted into a castle, and after¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards came into the possession of the Frangipani.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}*Thermae of Titus (PI. II, 26; open daily from 9 a.m. till sun¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}set ; admission 1 fr.; on Sundays gratis). Maecenas once possessed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a villa here, which was afterwards incorporated with the golden pa¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lace of Nero. On the site of the latter, in the year 80, Titus has¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tily erected his sumptuous Thermae, which were altered and enlarg¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed hy Domitian, Trajan, and others. The ruins are scattered over{% endraw %}
{% raw %}several vineyards, and a small part only, excavated in 1813, is ac¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cessible.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The earlier structure of Nero is easily distinguished from that of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Titus. The long vaulted parallel passages first entered belong to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thermse. They form together a semicircular substructure, the object of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which is not clearly ascertained. Most of the chambers beneath, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were filled up by Titus in the construction of his baths, and re-excavated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the beginning of the 16th cent., belonged to the golden palace of Nero.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A suite of seven rooms is first entered here; to the left, near that in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}centre, are remains of a spring. Traces of the beautiful Paintings, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}before the discovery of Pompeii were the sole specimens of ancient deco¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ration of this description, and served as models for Giovanni da Udine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Raphael in the decoration of the loggie, are still observed. Colon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nades appear to have flanked both sides of these rooms. A passage leads{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hence to a bath-room. To the left, at, right angles with this suite, are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a number of small and unadorned rooms, probably the dwellings of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}slaves; to the left again, opposite the first suite, is a passage once lighted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from above, the vaulting of which was adorned with beautiful frescoes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still partially visible.{% endraw %}
