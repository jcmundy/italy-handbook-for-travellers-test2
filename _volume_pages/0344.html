{% raw %}The street ascending to the right of the egress, past the arch of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Titus, leads to the monastery church of S. Sebastiano alia Polveriera{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(see Plan), the tribune of which contains mural paintings supposed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to date from the 6th century. The garden of the Franciscan monas¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tery of S. Bonaventura, situated higher up, with its conspicuous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palms, is a favourite point of view.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Velabrum and Forum Boarium.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Quitting the Forum, we now follow the slope of the Palatine,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}passing the church of S. Maria Liberatrice (PI. 11,20), which stands{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the site of the temple of Vesta; we then traverse the Via di S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Teodoro, and reach on the left the round church of S. Teodoro{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PI. II, 21), standing in a low situation, a little back from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}street. The earliest mention of it dates from the time of Gregory{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Great, and it probably occupies the site of an ancient temple.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the interior is preserved a Christian mosaic of the 7th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(The church is accessible on Fridays before 9 a. m.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}A little beyond it the street divides. That to the right, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we follow, descends to the ancient Velabrum, a quarter prolonged{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towards the Forum hy the Vicus Tuscus (p. 226), and towards the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}river by the Forum Boarium. The first ancient building we reach{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the so-called *Janus Quadrifrons (Arco di Giano; PI. II, 21),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an arched passage with four facades, dating from the later imperial{% endraw %}
{% raw %}age , and supposed to have been erected in honour of Constantine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Great. Above it once rose a second story, and it was perhaps{% endraw %}
{% raw %}used as a kind of exchange.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the right of this is S. Giorgio in Velabro (PI. II, 21), found¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed in the 4th cent., re-erected hy Leo II. in 682 and dedicated to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}SS. George and Sebastian, and often restored subsequently. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portico, according to the metrical inscription, dates from one of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these restorations. (In the middle ages the word Velabrum was altered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to 'velum aureum'.) The interior is a basilica with aisles, sixteen{% endraw %}
{% raw %}antique columns, and an old tabernacle. The frescoes of Giotto with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which the tribuna is said to have been once adorned have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painted over. Festivals, 20th Jan. and 23rd April. (The church is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}generally closed ; visitors knock at the door by the church to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left, behind the arch mentioned below.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}Adjacent to the church is the small Arch of the Money-changers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}( Arms Argentarius; PI. II, 21, /), which, according to the inscrip¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion, was erected by the money-changers and merchants of the Forum{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Boarium in honour of Septimius Severus and his wife and sons.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The worthless sculptures represent victims and sacrificial utensils.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From this point to the Tiber, stretched the extensive Forum{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Jioarium, or cattle-market, a very important centre of business.{% endraw %}
