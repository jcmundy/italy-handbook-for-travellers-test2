{% raw %}II. The Hills of Rome.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Quirinal. Viminal. Esquiline.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The following description embraces the E. part of Rome, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extends over the three long, parallel hills of the Quirinal, Vimi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nal, and Esquiline, and adjoins the Corso and Strangers' Quarter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the greater part of which is as yet occupied by vineyards and gar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dens, especially towards the walls. Since the Italian occupation,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}however, buildings are springing up here in every direction, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the Esquiline, in particular, whole quarters are being erected.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the Quirinal at a very early period lay a Sabine town, from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}union of which with that on the Palatine was formed the city of Rome.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Servian wall ran from the Capitol along the N.W. side of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Quirinal, and then to the E. behind the Baths of Diocletian and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church of S. Maria Maggiore, thus enclosing the Quirinal, Viminal, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}part of the Esquiline. According to the new division of the city made{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Augustus, this quarter comprised two districts, the Alta Semita{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Quirinal) and the Exquiliae (Esquiline). The construction of the wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Aurelian shows that this quarter was afterwards extended. According{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the mediaeval division these districts formed a single region only,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}named the Rione Monti, the most spacious of all the fourteen quarters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the city, as it extended from the Porta Pia to the now closed Porta{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Metronia, below the Lateran, and to the Forum Romanum. Its inhabi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tants , who were called Montigiani, differ, like those of Trastevere, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}some of their characteristics from the other Romans. The hill was pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vided with water by Sixtus V., by whom the long main street from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pincio to S. Maria Maggiore was also constructed. The second street in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}importance, intersecting the main street, and leading from the Piazza{% endraw %}
{% raw %}del Quirinale to the Porta Pia (Via del Quirinale and Via Venti Settembre),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was constructed by Pius IV.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the Piazza della Trinita on the Pincio (p. 143), crossing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the heights of the Quirinal and Viminal, a street 1 M. in length in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tersects this quarter of the town in a S. E. direction as far as the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church of S. Maria Maggiore on the Esquiline, the first part of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is called Via Sistina and the remaining portion Via delle Quatlro{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fontane. This street with its offshoots is at first well peopled,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}both with citizens and visitors, but beyond the Quirinal it be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}comes deserted, and building operations have only recently been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}begun here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Via Sistina (PI. I, 20) descends gradually from the Pincio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Piazza Barberini (5 min.). The first cross-street descending{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the right is called Via di Capo le Case (p. 145); its prolongation{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the left is the Via di Porta Pinciana, which ascends to the gate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of that name (closed in 1808), and in which (left) is situated the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Villa Malta, once the property of King Lewis I. of Bavaria, and now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inhabited by German artists.{% endraw %}
