{% raw %}xviii{% endraw %}
{% raw %}CONVEYANCES.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Individuals who appeal to the generosity of the stranger, or to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their own honesty, or who, as rarely happens, are offended by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller's manifestation of distrust, may well be answered in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}words of the proverb, 'patti chiari, amicizia lunga\ The equanimity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the traveller's own temper will greatly assist him if involved in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a dispute or bargain, and he should pay no attention whatever to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vehement gesticulations or an offensive demeanour. The slighter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his knowledge of the Italian language is, the more careful should he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be not to involve himself in a war of words , in which he must ne¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cessarily be at a great disadvantage.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In a country where trifling donations are in constant demand, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller should always be provided with an abundant supply of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}copper coins. Drivers, guides, porters, donkey-attendants, etc. in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}variably expect, and often demand as their right a gratuity (buona{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mano, mancia, da bere, bottiglia, caffe, fumata), in addition to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hire agreed on, varying according to circumstances from 2-3 sous to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a franc or more. The traveller need not scruple to limit his dona¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tions to the smallest possible sums, as liberality is often a source{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of annoyance and embarrassment. Thus if half-a-franc is bestowed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where two sous would have sufficed, the fact speedily becomes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}known, and the donor is sure to be besieged by numerous other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}applicants whose demands it is impossible to satisfy.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The demeanour of the stranger towards the natives should be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}somewhat modified in accordance with their various natural char¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acteristics. The Italians of the North resemble the inhabitants of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the South of France, and those of Italian Switzerland. The char¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acter of the Tuscans is more effeminate, their language and man¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ners more refined. The bearing of the Roman is grave and proud.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}With these, the stranger will find no difficulty in associating; and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acts of civility or kindness will not be misplaced, even when con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ferred on persons of the lower orders.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}VII. Conveyances.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Railways. With the exception of the Bologna and Ancona line,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the whole of the railways in Central Italy belong to the Ferrovie{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Romane company. As already remarked in the first volume of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Handbook, the rate of travelling is very moderate, and the trains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are often behind time. The first class carriages are tolerably com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fortable, the second are inferior to those of the German railways,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and resemble the English and French, while the third class is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chiefly frequented by the lower orders. Among the expressions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with which the railway-traveller will soon become familiar are —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'pronW (ready), 'partenza' (departure), 'si cambia convoglio' (change{% endraw %}
{% raw %}carriages), and 'uscita (egress), which are shouted by the officials{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with characteristic vigour.{% endraw %}
