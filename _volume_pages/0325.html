{% raw %}**Colosseum (PI. II, 24), or, as it was originally called, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amphitheatrum Flavium, the largest theatre, and one of the most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}imposing structures in the world, completed by Titus in A.D. 80.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}It was inaugurated by gladiatorial combats , continued during 100{% endraw %}
{% raw %}days, in which 5000 wild animals were killed, and naval contests{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were exhibited ; and 87,000 spectators could be accommodated within{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its walls. The building has been known since the 8th cent, under{% endraw %}
{% raw %}its present name, derived probably from the colossal statue of Nero{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with which it was once adorned.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Having been injured by a fire in the reign of Macrinus, it was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}restored by Alexander Severus. in 248 the Emp. Philip here celebrated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 1000th anniversary of the foundation of Rome with magnificent games.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In 405 gladiator-combats were abolished by Honorius as inconsistent with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the spirit of Christianity, but wild-beast fights continued down to the time{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Theodoric the Great. In the Middle Ages the Colosseum was employed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the Roman barons, especially the Frangipani, as a stronghold. In 1312{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Annibaldi were compelled to surrender it to the Emperor Henry VII.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who presented it to the Roman senate and people. In 1332 the Roman no¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bility again introduced bull-fights. After this period, however, the destruction{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Colosseum began, and the stupendous pile began to he regarded as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a kind of quarry. In the 15th cent. Paul II. here procured the materials{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the construction of the Pal. di S. Marco (di Venezia), Card. Riario{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the Cancelleria, and Paul III. (1534-49) for the Palazzo Farnese.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sixtus V. proposed to establish a cloth-manufactory here, and Clement XL{% endraw %}
{% raw %}actually used the building as a salt-magazine. Benedict XIV. (1740-58){% endraw %}
{% raw %}was the first to protect the edifice from farther demolition by consecrating{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the interior to the Passion of Christ, on account of the frequency with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which the blood of martyrs had flowed there , and erecting small chapels{% endraw %}
{% raw %}within it, which were removed in 1874. The following popes, parti¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cularly Pius VII. and Leo XII., have averted the imminent danger of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fall of the ruins by the erection of huge buttresses. The steps in the interior{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were restored by Pius IX.{% endraw %}
