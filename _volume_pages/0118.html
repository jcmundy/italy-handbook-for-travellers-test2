{% raw %}The greatest depth, formerly 30-40 ft., is now 20 ft. only. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}15th cent, a drain (emissarius) conducted the water into a tribu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tary of the Tiber. In ancient times the area of the lake appears{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to have been smaller. A project for draining it entirely, formed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Napoleon L, is still frequently canvassed.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The reminiscence of the sanguinary victory which Hannibal gained{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here over the Roman consul C. Flamiuius in May, B.C. 217, imparts a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tinge of sadness to this lovely landscape. It is not difficult to reconcile{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the descriptions of Livy (22, 4 et seq.) and Polybius (3, S3 et seq.) with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the present appearance of the lake. In the spring of 217 Hannibal quitted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his winter-quarters in Gallia Cisalpina, crossed the Apennines, marched{% endraw %}
{% raw %}across the plains of the Arno, notwithstanding an inundation, devastating{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the country far and wide in his progress , and directed his course towards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the S., passing the Roman army stationed at Arezzo. The brave and able{% endraw %}
{% raw %}consul followed incautiously. Hannibal then occupied the heights which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surround the defile extending on the N. side of the lake from Borghetto to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Passignano, upwards of 5M. in length. The entrance at Borghetto, as well{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as the issue at Passignano, were easily secured. Upon a hill in the centre{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(site of the present Torre) his principal force was posted. A dense fog{% endraw %}
{% raw %}covered the lake and plain , when in the early morning the consul, igno¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rant of the plan of his enemy, whom he believed to be marching against{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rome, entered the fatal defile. When he discovered his error, it was too{% endraw %}
{% raw %}late; his entire left flank was exposed, whilst his rear was attacked by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the hostile cavalry from Borghetto. No course remained to him but to force{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a passage by Passignano, and the vanguard of 6000 men succeeded in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}effecting their egress (but on the following day were compelled to sur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}render). The death of the consul rendered the defeat still more disastrous.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Romans lost 15,000 men, while the remaining half of the army was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}effectually dispersed; and the Roman supremacy in Italy began to totter.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The slaughter continued for three hours. From the Gualandro two small{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brooks fall into the lake. One of these, crossed by the road, has been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}named Sanguinetlo in reminiscence of the streams of blood with which it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was once discoloured.{% endraw %}
