{% raw %}in his lifetime and under his direction. GiulioRomano(1493-1546){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Francesco Penni (1488-1528) had a considerable share in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painting of the Hall of Constantine; the completion of the Loggia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paintings was entrusted to them, Perino del Vaga (1499-1547),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Raffaello dal Colle, and others. For the decorative ornamentation of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Loggie and the Farnesina the master engaged the services of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Giovanni da Vdine (1487-1564). Romano exhibits himself most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}clearly as a pupil of Raphael in the Villa Madama Giulio, less{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so in his Madonnas (Pal. Colonna and Borghese).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The crowd of Architects, who appeared in Bramante's time,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}showed greater independence: Baldassare Peruzzi (1481-1563), who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}built the Farnesina and Pal. Massimi, Raphael himself and Giulio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Romano (Villa Madama), Antonio da San Gallo the younger, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whom originated the Pal. Farnese and a new plan for St. Peter's,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and lastly Michael Angelo , whose influence, gradually deposing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bramante, irresistibly impelled the architecture of Rome into new{% endraw %}
{% raw %}courses. And just as in Plastic art he scornfully rejects the recognised{% endraw %}
{% raw %}forms and forces upon them a new construction, in like manner as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Architect he concerns himself little about the accurate adjustment of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}subordinate parts, intent rather upon the effect to be produced by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}structure as a whole — usually one of ponderous immensity. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colonnades in the Palazzo Farnese, the conversion of the Baths of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Diocletian into the church of S. Maria degli Angeli — a work subse¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quently spoiled — and the Porta Pia are among his chief works. His{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief merit consists in his having reverted to the plans of Bramante{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the completion of St. Peter's, which since 1546 had been under{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his superintendence. The Cupola at least was carried out according{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to his designs , but the ground-plan, to the injury of the building,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was much altered, and the Latin substituted for the Greek Cross.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}As long as the'divine'Michael Angelo lived, Rome was so dazzled{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the splendour of his renown that no one suspected the Decline{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Art was at hand. In fact, however, it had already declared itself{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the death of Raphael. Rome once more captured and pillaged;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}orthodoxy reinstated; the church recoiling from the taint of Hu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manism: these were incisive events in the history of art, which now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}received a more distinctively ecclesiastical direction. The Foreign{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occupation of Rome expelled a vast number of her artists and laid a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chasm in the traditions of her art. As she once more recovered herself{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and under the pontificate of Sixtus V. (Felice Peretti, 1585-90) was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to all appearance again invested with her pristine grandeur, the en¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}couragement of art was revived, but in a spirit which presently pervaded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and brought into subjection every phase of art. To Sixtus V. the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Eternal City, which'forthwith doubled itself, owes her present aspect.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Acqua Felice, the Spanish Staircase, the Via Sistina, the Piazza{% endraw %}
{% raw %}di S. Giovanni in Laterano, the Obelisk in the Piazza of St. Peter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the restoration of the Columns of Trajan and Marcus Aurelius are his{% endraw %}
