{% raw %}The area of the Palatine is now occupied by two recentiy dissolved{% endraw %}
{% raw %}religious houses, the monastery of S. Bonaveniura, opposite the arch of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Titus, and the Villa Mills, once a nunnery of the order of St. Francis de{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sales; by three vineyards, the Vigna JTussiner on the N.W. side, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vigna del Collegio Inglese at the S. W. corner, and the Vigna di S. Se¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bastiano on the S.; and finally by the Orti Farnesiani, which cover the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whole of the N. E. part of the hill. These gardens were laid out by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Paul III. Farnese, who purposed to erect a magnificent villa here in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}style of the 16th century. Extensive excavations were begun here in 1726{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the superintendence of Bianchini, but the treasures of art found on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that occasion were afterwards transferred to Naples, and the place again{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entirely neglected. In 1861 Napoleon III. purchased the property from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}King Francis II. for 360,000 fr., and at a great expense caused the ruins{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the imperial palaces to be systematically excavated under the able{% endraw %}
{% raw %}superintendence of the architect Comm. Pietro Rosa. The Vigna Nussiner{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was presented to the city by the Emperor of Russia in 1857, after he had{% endraw %}
{% raw %}caused excavations to be made in it during the preceding nine years;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and since 1866 important discoveries have also been made by the cVii-.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Visconti in the Vigna del Collegio Inglese, which was purchased by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pius IX. Since the annexation of Rome to the kingdom of Italy, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}purchase of the Farnese Gardens by the Italian government in Dec. 1870,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for a sum of 650,000 fr., all these excavations have been entrusted to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sole management of M. Rosa. Notwithstanding the great difficulties{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which have attended the prosecution of the work, the rubbish being{% endraw %}
{% raw %}20 ft. deep at places, very important topographical discoveries have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}made here, although as yet few works of art have been found. The cha¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}racter of the ruins brought to light cannot always be precisely ascer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tained, but they convey a striking idea of the structures with which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Palatine was once covered.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The excavations are open to the public daily; comp. p. 118. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ruins may be inspected in the course of an afternoon, but their imposing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}character, coupled with the beautiful and varied views commanded by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Palatine, renders them well worthy of repeated visits.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}M. Rosa has drawn a Plan of the entire region, which is reproduced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}photographically and exposed to view at different points. — Permission to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sketch and take measurements is given by M. Contigliozzi, Capo dell1{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ufficio Tecnico della Direzione Generate delle Antichita, to whom an in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}troduction should be obtained.{% endraw %}
