{% raw %}Sabine Mts. SUBIACO. Environs of Rome. 369{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Subiaco(*LaPernice, pens. 5fr.) is a small town(6000inhab.) of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mediaeval appearance, commanded by a castle which was formerly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}often occupied by the popes. This was the ancient Sublaqueum,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situated in the territory of the ^Equi, and is now the capital of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Comarca. It sprang up on the grounds of an extensive villa of Nero,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was embellished by three artificial lakes, the 'Simbruina{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stagna' of Tacitus (Ann. 14, 22), which were destroyed by an inun¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dation in 1305, and have given the village its name. On the left side{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Anio, opposite the monastery of S. Scolastica, are seen walls{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and terraces of the time of Nero, who, according to Tacitus, narrowly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}escaped being struck by lightning whilst taking a repast here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The environs are delightful, and the far-famed monasteries{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(closed 12-3 p. m.) are extremely interesting. Guide not absolutely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}necessary. The excursion requires about 3 hrs., and affords a suc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cession of beautiful views. We follow the main street which inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sects the town, upwards of t/2 M. in length, and afterwards ascends{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the right bank of the Anio. About 3/4 M. from the last houses{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the suburb of S. Martino, before the road crosses the gorge by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the lofty bridge, a path supported by masonry ascends to the left,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}passing several chapels, and leading to the (V4 hr.) —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*Monasterif,s of S. Scolastica, of which there are three. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first was founded in 530 by St. Benedict, who retired to this spot,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and took up his abode as a hermit in one of the grottoes, now con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}verted into chapels (II Sagro Speco). The monastery was after¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards confirmed in its possessions liy Gregory I. and his successors.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the 7th cent, it was destroyed, in 705 rebuilt, and is now entirely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}modern. In 1052 a second monastery was erected, and a third{% endraw %}
{% raw %}added in 1235 by the Abbot Landus. (If time is limited the monas¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}teries should be visited in returning from the Sagro Speco.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}The First Monastery (entrance to the right in the passage, after the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}anterior court has been passed) possesses a few antiquities ; by the fountain{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a sarcophagus with Bacchanalian representations, handsome columns, etc.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}probably found during the erection of the building. The monastery for¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}merly possessed a library containing valuable MSS. In 1465 the German{% endraw %}
{% raw %}printers Arnold Pannartz and Conrad Schweinheim printed here the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}book published in Italy, an edition of Donatus, which was followed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lactantius, Cicero, and Augustine (1467), of which copies are still pre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}served here (see p. 202).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Second Monastery, dating from 1052, is one of the earliest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}specimens in Italy of the pointed style. The court contains a quaint{% endraw %}
{% raw %}relief and two mediaeval inscriptions.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Third Monastery, of 1235, contains an arcaded court decorated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with mosaic.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Church of S. Scolastica, originally founded by Benedict VII. in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}075, was completely modernised in the 18th cent., and now contains nothing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}worthy of note, excepting the fine carved choir-stalls.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}huge mass of stone, and shaded by oaks.{% endraw %}
