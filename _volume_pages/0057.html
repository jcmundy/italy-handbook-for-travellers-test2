{% raw %}names, Bramante, Michael Angelo, Raphael, everlasting beacons in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the path of art — three men who in the course of the 16th century{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(cinquecento) raised modern art to its loftiest pitch of splendour.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}His successor Leo X. (1513—1522) of the house of Medici owes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it to his lineage only that he should have transmitted to posterity so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}splendid a reputation,—that his name should be associated insepa¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rably with the greatest triumphs of art in modern times. Leo X.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inherited the well earned fame of his predecessor, but knew not how{% endraw %}
{% raw %}either to value or to use his inheritance aright. It was not given him{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to sway the imperious temper of Michael Angelo, nor fully to com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prehend the mighty schemes of Bramante. The latter's chief work,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the rebuilding of St. Peter's, can be adequately studied only in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}collection of original drawings in Florence which set forth the gran¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deur of Bramante's designs in all their completeness ; for so many{% endraw %}
{% raw %}different hands were employed in giving effect to these, that little{% endraw %}
{% raw %}remains of the original plan. Happily this little, viz. the dome with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the overwhelming impression of vastness it conveys, is of the very{% endraw %}
{% raw %}best. Bramante contemplated a central structure in the form of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greek cross, rounded at its extremities, which, crowned by a gigantic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dome, should present an ensemble at once simple and majestic.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Succeeding generations have failed to embody Bramante's ideal.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}His career, extending probably from 1444 to 1514, is involved in ob¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scurity. Of his works, Rome possesses numerous examples. The cir¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cular chapel in the monastery of S. Pietro in Moniorio, the court{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of >S. Maria della Pace, the arcades in the first court of the Vatican{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Cortile di S. Dainaso), the Palazzo Giraud and above all the Cancel-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leria are perfect examples of Renaissance.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We are wont to wonder at the profusion and splendour, too, of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}works to which the cinquecento gave birth. How much richer, how{% endraw %}
{% raw %}much more splendid would have been this profusion , had only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these works been carried out as originally designed by the artist:s{% endraw %}
{% raw %}creative genius !{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The same fatality which pursued Bramante's mightiest projects{% endraw %}
{% raw %}served to mar Michael Angelo's (1475-1564) supreme effort in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}realm of Plastic Art. The Tomb of Julius II., begun while that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pope was still living, was to consist of a large detached edifice with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}statues ol'Moses, St. Paul, and other colossal figures at its projecting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}angles, and ranged along its wall the naked forms of men in chains.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The work, however, soon came to a standstill, and at last, 30 years{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after its commencement (1545), it was placed in the church of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Pietro inVinooli where it now stands, deplorably, a mere fragment{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the original design. Its most striking feature is the tremendous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figure of Moses, rising in wrathful indignation at the worship of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}golden calf, to denounce the idolatry of the Israelites. In addition{% endraw %}
