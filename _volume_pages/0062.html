{% raw %}ROMAN ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vatican we have his frescoes in the gay Villa Farnesina. On the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one hand we are awed by devotional fervour, sublime aspiration,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thought earnest and profound; on the other we find Art revelling in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the joys of life, each form radiant with an ecstasy of innocent mirth.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nevertheless it will cost no great effort to discern in the Farnesina{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frescoes the impress of Raphael's genius. He was indebted for his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}version of the myth of Cupid and Psyche to a work of Apuleius, fa¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}miliar to readers of the 16th century as it had been to the Romans{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of old. Probably no author either in ancient or modern times can{% endraw %}
{% raw %}boast a more captivating illustration than Apuleius, while at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}same time none has been more freely handled by his illustrator.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In Raphael's hands the myth is moulded anew. Remembering that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it was the adornment of a festive chamber he had in hand, Raphael{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sedulously avoided everything repugnant to the festive mood. Pysche's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}woes were consigned to the background; the painter is intent upon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recording her triumphs only. The confined space afforded by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chamber serves only to stimulate the Artist's mastery of form. Ra¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}phael's representation of the myth is condensed: many scenes are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but glanced at for a moment, though essentials never escape him;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thus the claims of narration and decoration are adjusted without{% endraw %}
{% raw %}restraint. Harmony alike in idea and form; nobility of proportion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}never overstepping the bounds of refinement; the power of so losing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}himself in his subject as to present it devoid of individual caprice:{% endraw %}
{% raw %}attributes characteristic of Raphael as these are declare themselves{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the frescoes of the Farnesina as unmistakably as in the wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}paintings of the Vatican. The spectator's own unassisted eye will{% endraw %}
{% raw %}not fail to see that the pictures on the ceiling of the principal saloon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are far inferior in execution to the so-called Galatea in the neigh¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bouring apartment. He will find nevertheless that both are such as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}will reward careful study with the highest gratification — a delight{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it must be a lasting desire to renew.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The inaccessibility of the upper rooms of the Farnesina, adorned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Bazzi of Siena (1477-1549), commonly known as Sodoma, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his painting of the Nuptials of Alexander with Roxane, cannot be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}too much regretted. In the embodiment of sensuous grace and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beauty, Raphael found in Sodoma a worthy rival.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Sibyls of Raphael in S. Maria della Pace (1514) we find{% endraw %}
{% raw %}him competing •—• if the expression may be allowed — in another{% endraw %}
{% raw %}field. Here he trenches upon the domain of Michael Angelo ; not,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}however, that he is for a moment betrayed into disingenuousness by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contact with a presence so overpowering, or that is he beguiled into{% endraw %}
{% raw %}assuming a style foreign to his genius. True to himself, he accepts the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}limits prescribed by his subject, and combines an air of pleasing sere¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nity and infinite grace with the expression of prophetic inspiration.{% endraw %}
