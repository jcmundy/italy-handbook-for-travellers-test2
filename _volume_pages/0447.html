{% raw %}The great majority, however, of the paintings represent scenes sym¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bolical of the doctrines and hopes of Christianity. That of most frequent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recurrence is the Resurrection, typified either by the raising of Lazarus,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who appears at a door wrapped in his grave-clothes, while Christ, represented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beardless, stands before it with a wand, or by the history of Jonah sitting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the gourd, the prophet swallowed by the whale, and his final escape.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Good Shepherd also frequently appears, with the sheep which he has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recovered on his shoulders, sometimes surrounded by lambs, to whom the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apostles preach, and whose postures are expressive of the different spirit in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which they receive the word (e. g. catacombs of St. Calixtus). Abraham's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sacrifice, Noah in the ark, and the three men in the fiery furnace belong{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the same category.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Daniel among the lions is another favourite subject, and he is generally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}represented with his hands raised in prayer, an attitude in which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deceased themselves are often depicted. This is doubtless in allusion to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the frequently cherished hope that the deceased, especially the martyrs,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}would intercede for their bereaved friends. Moreover, in addition to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}words 'Requiescat' and 'in Pace', such appeals as 'Pray for thy husband,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for thy son', not unfrequently occur. Such views as these also serve to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}account for the great value which some of the early Christians attached{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to being interred near a martyr's grave.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lastly we find that many of the principal representations, in which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there is a great similarity of style, are connected with the sacraments of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}baptism and the communion. We observe here the same symbolical man¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ner of representation as in the others already mentioned, though the al¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lusion is often of a somewhat remote and general 'character. Thus, for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}example, in addition to the simple ceremony of baptism, Moses is frequently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}represented in the act of striking the rock, and the water being eagerly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}drunk by the thirsty Israelites. The fish, too, by a kind of acrostic,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}formed an important Christian symbol, as the Greek txd'vg (fish) consists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the initial letters of: lijoovs XQimbi Qeov Ylog Siod'jo (Jesus Christ{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Saviour, Son of God). The communion is generally portrayed as an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}assembly of persons, generally seven, around a table, on which, besides{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the bread, also lies a fish, again containing an allusion to Christ. Com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bined with this, a reference to the miracle of the loaves also frequently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appears in the form of baskets with loaves standing on the ground, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in other cases the event is literally represented. These subjects and many{% endraw %}
{% raw %}others, especially the traditions of the Old Testament which contain a typi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cal reference to New Testament history, recur continually in the paintings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the catacombs and in the sculptures on the ancient Christian sarcophagi.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The numerous inscriptions corresponding to these were, as already men¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tioned, of a very simple description down to the middle of the 3rd cent.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after which they become more lengthy, and contain more elaborate ejacula¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tions of grief and hope. — For purposes of study, the collection of pic¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tures, inscriptions, and sarcophagi in the Christian museum of the Lateran{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 273) will be found indispensable.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The catacombs extend around the city in a wide circle, the major¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ity, however, being concentrated between the Via Salara, the Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nomentana, the Via Latina, the Via Appia, and the Via Ostiensis.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Upwards of sixty different catacombs, varying greatly in extent, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only partially accessible, have been discovered. According toMichele{% endraw %}
{% raw %}de Rossi's careful calculations, they cover an area of 615 acres. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}order, however, to form an accurate idea of their extent, it must he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}borne in mind that the passages run one above another, as many as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}five being sometimes thus disposed. The highest of these lie 22-25{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ft. below the surface of the earth, while the lowest are 40-50 ft.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deeper. If the whole of these subterranean passages were placed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in a continuous line, their total length would be about 545 English{% endraw %}
