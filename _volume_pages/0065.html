{% raw %}ROMAN ART.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}appeared amongst the rival aspirants to fame in Rome. They sup¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}planted the Naturalists, appropriating as much of the latters' method{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as appeared available, and finally monopolised the favour of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}court and aristocracy. Nor was the struggle by any means confined{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the pallette and the brush. Personalities arose, and amongst them¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}selves the partisans of Caracci were seldom at peace. Their contri¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}butions are in part, at any rate, of the highest excellence. Anni-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bale Caracci's frescoes in the Palazzo Farnese; Guido Reni's Aurora{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the Casino Rospigliosi; the frescoes of Domenichino in S. Luigi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dei Francesi, S. Andrea della Valle, in Grotta Ferrata near Rome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are not mere master-pieces of technical skill, but are replete with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}artistic beauty and vitality.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Neapolitan sculptor Lorenzo Bernini (1598-1680) flourishes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}up to the close of the 17th century. His works occupy the concluding-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chapter in the history of Roman Art. It is superfluous to bid the be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}holder beware of being led captive by art essentially flimsy and mere¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tricious ; rather perhaps it is necessary, as a set off against the now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prevailing depreciation of Bernini's works, to plead the important{% endraw %}
{% raw %}historical significance they possess amidst all their too conspicuous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}defects; to bear in mind that throughout the course of nearly a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}century they were regarded as the most brilliant production of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that period and were very generally imitated.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Since the 17th century, Rome has not given birth to nor nur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tured any distinctive art life, though the past has held Artists of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}all nations spell-bound, compelling the conviction that Rome is still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the true High School of Art, whose teaching is indispensable to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}every true Artist. So late as the close of the 18th and the beginning{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the present century, Rome continued to give proofs of the potency{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of her influence. Without the suggestions which Rome alone could{% endraw %}
{% raw %}furnish, David would never have received that classical impulse{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which he turned to such admirable account in France. In the absence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of such inspiration as the spectacle of Rome's master-pieces alone can{% endraw %}
{% raw %}afford, Cornelius and his associates would never have had the courage{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to attempt the revival of fresco painting. Thus is it that Rome reacts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the destinies of modem art, though without an art life she can{% endraw %}
{% raw %}call her own. The statue of the Immaculata shows that the Romans{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are ignorant of the mere mechanism of casting in bronze, while the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frescoes of Podesti in the Stanza dell' Incendio prove that not only{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painting is a lost art, but Raphael himself is forgotten. Otherwise{% endraw %}
{% raw %}opinion had not been thus pointedly challenged. But if Rome has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceased to be the home as well as the birthplace of Art, it is above all{% endraw %}
{% raw %}others the spot where Art at once affords the highest gratification{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and commands the profoundest reverence.{% endraw %}
