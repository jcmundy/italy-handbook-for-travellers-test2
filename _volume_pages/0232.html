{% raw %}The choir of the church adjoins on the S. the Collegio Romano{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PI. II, 16), formerly a well attended Jesuit establishment, where{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the higher branches of classics, mathematics, philosophy, etc. were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}taught, and degrees conferred. The extensive building was erected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the end of the 16th cent., in the pontificates of Gregory XIII. and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sixtus V , by B. Ammanati. The massive principal facade looks{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towards the Piazza del Collegio Romano. A number of the rooms{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are occupied by the new Biblioteca Vittorio Emanuele, the nucleus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of which consists of the old Library of the Jesuits (63,000 vols, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2000 MSS.), augmented by the libraries of numerous suppressed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monasteries (in all about 450,000 vols, and several thousand MSS. ].{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Special attention is devoted to the department of modern literature.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The library (adm., see p. 109) possesses a well-arranged reading{% endraw %}
{% raw %}room, and is connected by a bridge with the Biblioteca Casanatense{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 198). — The building also contains the Liceo Ennio Quirino{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Visconti, the new Museo Preistorico, and the *Museo Kiiicheriano,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}founded by the learned Athanasius Kircher, born in 1601, a Jesuit{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and teacher at Wtirzburg in 1618, afterwards professor of mathe¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}matics in the Coll. Romano, and celebrated for his mathematical and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scientific discoveries (d. 1680). The museum is now government{% endraw %}
{% raw %}property, and is open daily, 9-3; admission 1 fr., on Sundays gra¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tis. The entrance is in the Via del Collegio Romano 216, by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}door facing the visitor (where there are several inscriptions and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seven colossal horses' heads in the passage); we ascend by a stair¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}case to the left to the 2nd floor; here we turn to the right round{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the corner, and finally ascend a spiral staircase at the end of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}corridor.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On entering we turn to the left into an Ante-Chamber, which contains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a few busts and heads, two triangular bases for candelabra, and a small{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sarcophagus. — We then turn again to the left, leaving the (right) ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}joining series of rooms containing inscriptions, etc., till afterwards.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Room I. contains models of columbaria, cinerary urns, inscriptions, etc.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Spain to Rome; small vessels of terracotta with inscriptions scratched upon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}them; leaden balls of Roman slings. At the wall, to the right, is a{% endraw %}

{% raw %}bianic Ligurians (near Benevcnto), referring to the alimentary laws of the{% endraw %}
