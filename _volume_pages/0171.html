{% raw %}The high-road which connects Rome with the Valley of the Po traverses{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Umbrian plains of Terni and Spoleto, and then ascends the valley of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Topino and the Chiascio, until it reaches its culminating point 011 the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Apennines. Descending on the E. side of that range, it follows the course{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Metaurus to its mouth at Fano , after which it skirts the coast and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leads N. to Bologna and the valley of the Po. It is identical with the an¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cient Via Flaminia, constructed in B.C. 220 by the Censor C. Flaminius{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(who afterwards fell at the Battle of the Trasimene Lake, see p. 46), in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}order to secure the possession of the district of the Po which had been at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that time wrested from the Gauls. This road is still one of the most im¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portant channels of local traffic in Central Italy, but since the completion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Apennine Railway from Bologna to Florence, and the line recently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}opened from Ancona to Rome (R. 14), has been little frequented by tourists.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}It is, however, replete with natural attractions, and affords the traveller{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an opportunity of becoming acquainted with several towns which merit a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}visit on account of their monuments and historical associations.{% endraw %}
