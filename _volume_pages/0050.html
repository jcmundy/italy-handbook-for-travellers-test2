{% raw %}ROMAN ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}several centuries the towns of Tuscany were the principal abodes of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a natural art life. But just as in Rome Italian art achieved its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most signal triumphs in the persons of Raphael and Michael Angelo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the masters of thatperiod : so in Roman ground we find that Chris¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tian art first took root and attained to its most important dimen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sions. In Rome then we find the strongest inducements as well as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the richest opportunity for the study of Early Christian Art.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the4th century heathendom, long tottering to its fall, was, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appearance at least, absorbed in the younger Christian world. A new{% endraw %}
{% raw %}era in art is inaugurated. Not that we are to assume the simulta¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}neous extinction of the pagan art of ancient Rome , nor that it was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at once superseded by an altogether new style provided as it were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the emergency. The eye and hand are to a greater extent crea¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tures of habit than the mind. New views and altered conceptions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Supreme Being as well as of the destiny of man found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acceptance. But to embody them the artist had to resort to the old{% endraw %}
{% raw %}established forms. Then heathen rules were by no means uni¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}formly hostile to Christianity (the period of bitterest persecution{% endraw %}
{% raw %}began with the 3rd century A. D.); and that the new doctrine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}should have expanded and taken root, should have been permitted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to organise itself in the very midst of heathen society , is evidence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that it was received even with favour.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}As a consequence of these conditions it will be observed that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the art of the early Christians presents no remarkable con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trast to that which precedes it, and that they were content to adopt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and perpetuate the traditions of the antique. The Roman Cata¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}combs afford abundant proof of this. Encircling the city as with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a subterranean trench, they were originally far from being what{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they subsequently became — secret, carefully concealed places of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}refuge for the early Christians; but rather their regularly ordained{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and publicly accessible places of burial (e. g. the Catacomb of Nico-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}medus and that of Flavia Domitilla), and were first designedly con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}signed to darkness and concealment during the 3rd century, a period{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of constantly recurring persecution. The Christian community,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reared as it was in the midst of Roman paganism, probably did{% endraw %}
{% raw %}not dream of subverting the principles of antique art. In the adorn¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment of the Catacombs they retain the types transmitted to them;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so also in the particulars of drawing and colour the precedent of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Antique is closely followed. Christ represented as the Good{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shepherd, Orpheus as the symbol of Christ, and evidences of the long{% endraw %}
{% raw %}standing repugnance to any rendering of the Passion-history, afford{% endraw %}
{% raw %}proofs of the readiness to accept the art heritage of their precursors.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}technical rendering of form, from the typical reliefs found on pagan{% endraw %}
