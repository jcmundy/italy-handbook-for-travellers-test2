{% raw %}frequently placed earthen lamps, partly as symbols of the resurrection,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and perhaps also for practical purposes, just as lamps had always been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}much used in the heathen observance of worshipping the dead.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The system of monotonous series of passages was sometimes broken{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the introduction of larger chambers, which were either used as cubi-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cula, or family burial-places, or as vaults for the interment of the mar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tyrs or certain ecclesiastical dignitaries. An example of the last case{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is afforded by the catacombs of Calixtus, destined for the remains of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}popes. Finally we also find chambers that were set apart for the celebra¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion of divine worship. Although it is erroneous to suppose that this was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the original object of the catacombs, divine service being doubtless per¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}formed in private houses in the city, it is well ascertained that, from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 2nd cent., the Christians occasionally assembled at the graves of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}martyrs for the purposes of prayer and the celebration of the communion.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}These meetings took place on the anniversaries of the deaths of martyrs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or other believers, to whose memory 'agapge' or love-feasts were celeb¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rated. These were customs analogous to the heathen rites in honour of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the deceased, but in times of persecution the whole community was obliged{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to seek refuge in these subterranean cavities, and the construction of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}larger Chapels was thus rendered necessary. These were usually made by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the formation of a cavity on each side of the passage, one for each sex,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in accordance with the rule of the early church which required the sep¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aration of the sexes during divine worship. The tomb of a martyr was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}then generally used as a tribune, in front of which an altar, often portable,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was erected. Light and air were in many cases admitted from above by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}means of 'luminaria'. Thus these chapels, containing, or in immediate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}proximity to, the tombs of the martyrs, formed, as it were, rallying points{% endraw %}
{% raw %}throughout the entire system, and, as they continued to be objects of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}veneration long after the catacombs were disused as burial-places, they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were often at a later period rendered accessible by the construction of stairs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}III. Decoration of the Catacombs. This is one of their most inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}esting features. Christian art in its origin could, of course, be but an appli¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cation of ancient precepts to the new objects and conceptions introduced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the new religion. The paintings and sculptures of the catacombs are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}therefore in no respect different in style from contemporaneous works, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with them shared in the precipitate and almost total degradation of art.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The best frescoes belong to the end of the 1st and beginning of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}2nd century. With the general decline of the Roman empire in the 3rd{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and 4th cent., artistic forms became distorted and unpleasing, and in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}case of decorative works there is no difference in style between Christian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and heathen art, especially during the earlier periods.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the other hand, a peculiar significance in the choice and treatment{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the subjects is observable from the earliest period. Comparatively few{% endraw %}
{% raw %}historical paintings are met with, and these have no other object in view{% endraw %}
{% raw %}than the illustration of some simple fact from Jewish or Christian lore.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Occasionally a Madonna and Child are observed , generally with the Magi,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}varying in number, who present their offerings, as in the catacombs of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}St. Calixtus, Domitilla, and Priscilla; and there are also a few scenes of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}martyrdom and other subjects.{% endraw %}
