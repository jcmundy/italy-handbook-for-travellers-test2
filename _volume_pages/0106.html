{% raw %}34 Route 5. SIENA. 8. Domenico.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}very picturesquely situated at the base of the hill of S. Domenico,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mentioned as early as 1081, renovated in 1198, and pTaised by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Dante (Inf. 30, 78: 'Per Fontebranda non darei la vista'). — The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Via di Fontebranda ascends to the Campo (on the left), and to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cathedral (on the right). •— Passing the fountain, and ascending to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right, we reach —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Domenico (PL D, 3, 4), a lofty brick edifice in the Gothic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}style (1220-1465), the massive substructions of which rest on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}slope of the hill, with a campanile dating from 1340.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Interior is destitute of aisles, and has a transept and open roof.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the entrance, to the right, is the Cappella delle Volte (closed), con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}taining a Madonna and saints by Girolamo di Benvenuto, 1508, and an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}altar-piece, St. Catharine by Andrea Vanni. — Farther on, to the right:{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Monument of the mathematician Gius. Pianigiani (d. 1850), by Bceheroni.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— Third altar: St. Peter the Martyr, by Salimbeni, 1570. — The 'Chapel{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of St. Catharine, in which the head of the saint is preserved in a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}silver reliquary enclosed in a shrine dating from 1466, is adorned with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}admirable frescoes by Sodoma. On tlie wall near the altar, St. Catharine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in ecstasy, supported by two sisters (the so-called 'Svenimento', or faint),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and an angel bringing her the host; on the wall to the left, The prayer{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the saint saving the soul of a decapitated culprit; to the right, Healing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the possessed, by Francesco Vanni, 1593. The two saints on the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and left of the entrance are by the same master; the ceiling was executed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Sodoma. — The pavement of the chapel is richly decorated with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}graffito representations on marble. — Last altar to the right: Nativity of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christ of the school of Franc, di Giorgio, executed under the influence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Luca Signorelli, to whom the work was formerly attributed; the upper{% endraw %}
{% raw %}part is probably by Matteo da Siena, the foreground by Fungai.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ciioir. The beautiful 'Marble Ciborium at the high-altar, hitherto{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ascribed by the Sienese to Michael Angelo , is more probably the work{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Benedetto da Majano. — A beautiful 'view of the lofty and imposing-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cathedral may be obtained from the window at the back of the high{% endraw %}
{% raw %}altar. — The 2nd Chapel to the left of the high altar contains a Ma¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}donna by Guido da Siena , an interesting picture, although the date 1221{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appears to be spurious (1284). To the right: SS. Barbara, Mary Magdalene,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Catharine by Matteo da Siena, 1479; in the lunette above, a Piefa by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Girolamo Benvenuto: the Madonna with saints to the left was executed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the same master, 1508; the lunette representing the Adoration of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Magi is by Matteo da Siena. — The 2nd Chapel to the right of the high{% endraw %}
{% raw %}altar contains numerous old tombstones with coats of arms, many of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which belong to Germans who studied at the university in the 15th and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}16th centuries.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We now return by the Via del Paradiso and the small Piazza{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Giuseppe Pianigiani, in which stands the little church of S. Maria{% endraw %}
{% raw %}delle Nevi (PL 6), with a handsome facade, and a good picture by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Matteo da Siena (Madonna with numerous saints, 1477), to the Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cavour (p. 25), which farther on contains several handsome pa¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}laces, such as Palazzo Mocenni, Ciaia, and others.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We next come to the small Piazza S. Petronilla on the right,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whence the Via Garibaldi leads to the Porta S. Lorenzo and the rail¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}way-station. — The streets to the left of Via Cavour open into the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lizza (PL E, 2, 3), a small promenade which was laid out in 1779{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the site of a former fortress erected by Charles V., commanding{% endraw %}
{% raw %}good views of S. Domenico and the Cathedral. Those walks extend as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}far as the entrance to Fort St. Barbera, built by Cosmo I. in 1560,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}open to the public and affording a good survey.{% endraw %}
