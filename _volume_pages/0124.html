{% raw %}Setting aside the painters of the 11th cent., who were dispersed among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}various small towns, we find that Vttaviauo Nelli of Gubbio (15th cent.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}was the first able representative of this school. Works by this master{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are preserved both at his native town and at Foligno. Nelli was, how¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ever, eclipsed by Gentile da Fabriano (b. about 1360-70), who probably{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had studied the Sienese masters in his youth, and who afterwards un¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dertook long journeys (e. g. to Venice and Rome), thus establishing his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reputation throughout Italy. His style not unfrequently resembles the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Flemish. Besides Gubbio and Fabriano, other Umbrian towns possessed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}local schools of painting, such as Cameriuo and Foligno. The latter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}about the middle of the 15th cent., gave birth to Niccolb Alunno, a man{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of limited ability, which, however, he cultivated to the utmost. His{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prevailing theme is the Madonna, to whose features he imparts beauty in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}happy combination with reverie; and in this department he may be re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}garded as the precursor of Perugino and Raphael.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Meanwhile Perugia , the largest city in this district, by no means{% endraw %}
{% raw %}remained idle. In this wider and more enterprising field the old con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ventional styles were soon abandoned as unsatisfactory, and the necessity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of adopting the Florentine style was urgently felt. In the latter half of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 15th century Benedetto Buonfigli was the first master who strove{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to throw aside the local style of painting , and the same effort was made{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Fiorenzo di Lorenzo, a younger master and perhaps a pupil of Bene¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}detto.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This improved style was brought to maturity by Pietro Vannucci of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Citta della Pieve (1446-1521), surnamed Perugino, after the chief scene{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of his labours, a master to whom the Umbrian school is chiefly indebted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for its fame. Perugia was, however, by no means the only sphere of his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}activity. He repeatedly spent years together in Florence, and was em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ployed for a considerable time in Rome. His endeavours to overcome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the defects of his native school were crowned with success. In Vcr-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rocchio's studio in Florence he was initiated into the secrets of perspec¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tive and the new mode of colouring, and in both respects attained con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}summate skill. Down to the beginning of the 16th cent, his excellence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}continued unimpaired, as his frescoes in the Cambio, and his Madonna{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and saints (No. 38) in the Gallery at Perugia sufficiently prove. During{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the last twenty years of his life, however , his works show a falling off,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occasioned, doubtlessly, by his accepting more orders than he could{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conscientiously execute, whereby his art was degraded to a mere handi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}craft. He seems, indeed, to have had more studios than one at the same{% endraw %}
{% raw %}time, as for example in 1502-5 both at Florence and Perugia, in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}latter of which the young Raphael was employed.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Another great master of" the Umbrian school, vying with Perugino,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is Bernarluno Betti, surnamed Pinturicchio (1454-1513). Although he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}exercised no considerable influence on the progress of Italian art, and in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}troduced no striking improvements like Leonardo, and others, yet he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}thoroughly understood how to utilise the traditional style and the cur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rent forms, and was marvellously prolific as a fresco painter. The Va¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tican and Human churches, the Cathedral library at Siena, and the Col¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}legiate church at Spello, are the chief scenes of his activity. — Amongst{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the younger contemporaries of Perugino we must next mention Giovanni{% endraw %}
{% raw %}di Pietro, surnamed Lo Spagua after his native country, whose paintings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are hardly inferior to the early works of Raphael, and who, in common{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with all the Umbrian masters, exhibits great ease of execution.{% endraw %}
