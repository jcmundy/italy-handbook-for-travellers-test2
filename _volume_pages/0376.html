{% raw %}great martyrdoms at Rome sprang up the Church of St. Peter in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}immediate neighbourhood of which paganism maintained its footing with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}greater obstinacy than in any other part of the city. Not tar from the{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Tomb on the bank of the river. This monument was afterwards converted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into a tete-de-pont, but at what date is uncertain. In 537 it effectually{% endraw %}
{% raw %}repelled the attacks of the Goths, and since that period it has constituted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the citadel of Rome, commonly called the Castle of S. Angelo, on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}possession of which the mastery over the city has always depended.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Around the Church of St. Peter sprang up a number of chapels, churches,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monasteries, and hospitals, and in the pontificate of Symmachus (496-514){% endraw %}
{% raw %}a papal palace also. Foreign pilgrims soon began to establish settlements{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here named scholae, or borghi, of which in the 8th cent, four are men¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tioned in history, viz. those of the Saxons (i.e. English), the Frisians,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Lombards, and the Franks, who in time of war formed separate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}companies of soldiers. In order to protect the whole of this region{% endraw %}
{% raw %}against the predatory incursions of the Saracens, Leo IV. surrounded it,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 848-52 with a wall 40 ft. in height, and thus became the founder{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Civitas Leonina named after him. This quarter of the city was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}repeatedly destroyed during the conflicts of the middle ages, as on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occasion of the retreat of Henry V. before Robert Guiscard in 1084, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}when the Castle of S. Angelo was destroyed by the Romans in 1379. A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}new era in the history of the Borgo began with the return of the popes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from Avignon; streets gradually sprang up; and the walls were con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}siderably extended. Eugene IV. and Sixtus IV. were particularly active{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in developing the Borgo, and it attained the height of its prosperity in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the pontificate of Julius II. and Leo X. at the beginning of the 16th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}century. The papal court, however, was unable permanently to attract{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the business nf the city to its neighbourhood, and a sparse and poor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}population, engaged in the humbler branches of trade, now lives beneath{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the shadow of the most famous church and the most imposing palace in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Christendom. Down to the pontificate of Sixtus V. the Borgo belonged{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the popes, and lay without the bounds of the municipal jurisdiction;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but that pope incorporated it with the city as a '7th Rione', and in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plebiscite of 2nd Oct. 1870 the inhabitants of the Borgo declared their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}desire that it should continue to form an integral part of Rome.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Angelo (PL 1,10), consisting of five arches, but originally of seven,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one next the land on each side being now built up. It was erect¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed by Hadrian in order to connect his tomb with the city in A.D.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}136, and named after him Pons &lius. At the 8. end of the bridge,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the site of two old chapels, Clement VII. erected statues of Pe¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ter by Lorenzetto, and Paul by Paolo Romano. The ten colossal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}statues of angels, formerly much admired, were executed from Ber¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nini's designs in 1688, and testify to the low ebb of plastic taste at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that period. One angel (fourth on the right, with the cross) is er¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}roneously ascribed to Bernini himself; two executed by him for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this bridge are now in S. Andrea delle Fratte (p. 145). The bridge{% endraw %}
{% raw %}commands a pleasing view of the Pincio with the Villa Medici.{% endraw %}
