{% raw %}37S Environs of Rome. GALERA. Etruscan Towns.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Veii was one of the most powerful of the Etruscan cities. After{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contests protracted for centuries, which at first centred round{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fidenae (Castel Giubileo. p. 350), the frontier stronghold of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Etruscans on the S. bank of the Tiber, and after manifold vicis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situdes and a long siege, the city was at length captured by Ca¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}millus, B.C. 396. Its circumference, which may still be traced,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is 51/.) M. After its capture it fell to decay, and was afterwards re-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}peopled by Caasar with Romans; but this colony scarcely occupied{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one-third of the former area. Excavations here have led to the dis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}covery of inscriptions, statues, and the columns which adorn the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}old post-office in the Piazza Colonna (p. 148).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Veii stands on a table-land, around which, on the N. and E.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sides, flows the ancient Cremera, now the Fosso di Formello, and on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the W. side, towards Isola, the Fosso dell' Isola. The ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}citadel, now the Piazza d'Armi, occupies a separate plateau at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}confluence of the two brooks, connected with the site of the town by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a narrow isthmus only. The camp of the Fabii, whose whole family{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was destroyed by the Veientines, was situated on the heights on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right bank of the Valca, as the Cremera is named in the lower{% endraw %}
{% raw %}part of its course, about iy2 M. distant from the citadel. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}* Grotta Campana, named after its discoverer, is the only tomb of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Veii still preserved, and is left in the condition in which it was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}found in 1842. It is hewn in the tufa-rock , and guarded by two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lions at the entrance. The interior consists of two chambers; the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walls are covered with grotesque paintings of great antiquity. Two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}skeletons were found here, but they soon fell to pieces. Remains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the armour of a warrior, vessels of clay, etc. are also seen.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Galera.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Galera, 15 M. from Rome, may be visited on the way to Bracciano,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or by carriage (about 25 fr.), in a single day from Rome. A supply of pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}visions necessary. Vetturini also occasionally run (see below).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About '/2M. beyond La Storta (p. 377) the Via Clodia diverges{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the left from the Via Cassia, which leads to Baccano (p. 69). We{% endraw %}
{% raw %}follow the Via Clodia, the old pavement of which is seen at intervals.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The district is dreary. On the road-side is the entrance to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}subterranean Acqua Paola, which descends from the lake of Brac¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ciano, and terminates on the Janiculus. On the left, 4'/2 M. from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}La Storta, appears the church of S. Maria di Cesareo; 1 M. farther{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the Osteria Nuova, where the carriage may be left. The land{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here is well watered, and occupied by several extensive farms. A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}path to the left in the direction of these, then turning to the right,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leads in '/._, hr. to the ruins of Galera. The town, which arose in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the middle ages near the Carciae of antiquity, was at first governed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by powerful nobles, and belonged to the Orsini 1226-1670. At the{% endraw %}

{% raw %}malaria to abandon the place. Even the solitary shepherd who now{% endraw %}
