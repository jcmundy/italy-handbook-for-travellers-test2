{% raw %}The Velia.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Alban, and to the left the Sabine Mts. To the S. the Palatine with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ruins of the imperial palaces and two monasteries, and the opposite bank{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Tiber with the Villa Pamfili. Towards the W. the Capitol; to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right of it, between the domes of two churches, Trajan's column is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}visible; above the latter Monte Mario; farther to the right the Torre di{% endraw %}

{% raw %}site of a temple of Venus and Roma (see below), is the church of —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Francesca Romana (PI. II, 23), or S. Maria Nuova, standing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the site of an older church of Nicholas I. founded about 860, re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}elected after a fire by Honorius III. about 1216, and modernised{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Carlo Lombardo in 1615. Festival, 9th March.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Interior. On the right, 2nd Chapel: (r.) Monument of Card. Vulcani{% endraw %}
{% raw %}I d. 1322) and that of the papal commandant and general Antonio Rido (d. 1475).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}3rd Chapel: Miracles of St. Benedict, altar-piece by Subleyras. In the{% endraw %}

{% raw %}(1.) SS. John and James, (r.) Peter and Andrew. Over the high-altar an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ancient Madonna, traditionally attributed to St. Luke, which is said alone{% endraw %}

{% raw %}monument of Gregory XI., who transferred the papal residence from Avignon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Rome (d. 1378), with a relief by Olivieri. Here on the right, built into{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the wall, are two stones on which Peter and Paul are said to have knelt{% endraw %}
{% raw %}when they prayed for the punishment of Simon Magus. In the Confessio a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}group of the saints with an angel, by Melt. Under the tribune (closed, but{% endraw %}

{% raw %}On the summit of the Velia, by the Palatine, rises the Trium¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}phal Arch of Titus, erected to commemorate the defeat of the Jews{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(A.D. 70), and dedicated to him under his successor Domitian in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}SI, as the inscription on the side next the Colosseum records: —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Senatus populusque Romanus divo Tito divi Vespasiani filio Vespasiano{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Augusto. The arch is embellished with fine reliefs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Outside : On the same side as the inscription, is a representation of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sacrificial procession on the frieze. Inside: Titus crowned by Victory in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a quadriga driven by Roma; opposite, the triumphal procession with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}captive Jews, table with the show-bread, and candlestick with seven{% endraw %}
{% raw %}branches. — In the middle ages the arch was used as a fortress by tlie{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Frangipani, crowned with battlements, and strengthened by new walls.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}When these were removed in 1822 under Pius VII., the arch lost its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}support, and had to be reconstructed, as the inscription on the other side{% endraw %}
{% raw %}informs us. The central part, composed of marble, is therefore alone{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ancient, while the restored parts are of travertine.{% endraw %}
