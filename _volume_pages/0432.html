{% raw %}Trastevere.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Janiculus (275 ft.) rises to a commanding height near the river,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and was on that account chosen by the ancient kings of Rome as the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}site of a castle, which they connected with the city lying opposite to it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by a doubie wall. The hill was at length annexed to the city by Augustus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as a 14th quarter, which he named the Regio Tronstiberina. The banks{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Tiber here were bordered with handsome villas, but the quarter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}always retained the character of a suburb, and was much frequented by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}foreigners, and particularly Jews, who formed a community here down{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the beginning of the 16th century. Trastevere is now inhabited almost{% endraw %}
{% raw %}exclusively by the working classes, among whom many well-built and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}handsome persons of both sexes will be observed. The inhabitants of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Trastevere maintain that, they are the most direct descendants of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ancient Romans, and their character and dialect differ in many respects{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from those of the citizens of other quarters.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Trastevere is connected with the city by three bridges, the most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}N. of which is the Ponte Sisto (PL II, 11), constructed hy Baccio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pintelli under Sixtus IV., in 1474, on the site of the Pons Aurelius,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was destroyed in the 8th century. Fine view from the bridge.{% endraw %}
