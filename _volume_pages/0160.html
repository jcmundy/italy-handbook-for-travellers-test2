{% raw %}52 M Cesena (Leon d'Oro, R. 2'/2 fr.; Cappella), with 10,000{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inhab. (inol. villages 35,000), surrounded by beautiful meadows{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and hills, and boasting of numerous palaces.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Caesena is one of the most ancient episcopal sees in Italy, where St.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Philemon is said to have held the office as early as the year 92. During{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the middle ages it was at first an independent state, then became subject{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Ghibelline family of Montefeltro, and shortly afterwards to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Malatestas, who where partizans of the Guelphs. This rapid change of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rulers is alluded to by Dante, Inf. xxvii, 52:{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cost com' ella sie" tra il piano e il monte,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tra tirannia si vive e stato franco.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On 1st Feb., 1377. the town was cruelly sacked by Cardinal Robert{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Geneva, and subsequently by Cesare Borgia, after which it was incor¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}porated with the States of the Church.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Piazza is the handsome Palazzo Pubblico with a statue{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Pius VI., who was born here in 1717, as well as his successor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pius VII. in 1742. In the picture-gallery a Presentation in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple, by Francesco Francia. — The Cathedral contains two marble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}altars of the 15th and 16th cent. — The Library, founded in 1452{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Domenico Malatesta Novello, contains 4000 MSS., many of them{% endraw %}
{% raw %}executed by order of the founder, and afterwards employed by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}learned Aldus Manutius in the preparation of his celebrated editions{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the classics.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On an eminence, 3/^ M. distant, stands the handsome church{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of *<S. Maria del Monte, a work of Bramante. Productive sulphur-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mines in the vicinity, towards the S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The line crosses the stream Pisciatello, the upper part of which,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}called Urgone, is identical with the Rubicon of the ancients, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}boundary between Italy proper and the province of Gallia Cisalpina,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and memorable for its passage by Ctesar at the beginning of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}civil war between him and Pompey, B.C. 49.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The most recent investigations tend to show that the Rubicon has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entirely quitted its ancient course. It appears originally to have fallen{% endraw %}
{% raw %}into the Fiuniicino, farther S., while at the present day its upper part{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Urgone) unites with the Pisciatello. Most of the towns and villages of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this district have in turn laid claim to the distinction of possessing the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rubicon within their territory. Nor did they rest satisfied with a mere{% endraw %}
{% raw %}literary feud in order to gain the object of their ambition. An action in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}volving this question was instituted at Rome, and in 1756 the 'Rota' de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cided in favour of the claim of the Uso (see below), beyond the small{% endraw %}
{% raw %}town of Savignano, and near S. Arcangelo (birthplace of pope Clement XIV.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ganganelli in 1705).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the road between Cesena and Savignano stands a column bearing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a decree of the Roman senate, threatening to punish those who should{% endraw %}
{% raw %}without authority trespass beyond the Rubicon. Montesquieu regarded this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as genuine, but it is an obvious imposition and not the only one connect¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed with the interminable dispute on the subject of the Rubicon.{% endraw %}
