{% raw %}10 Route ;>. VOLTERRA. From Leghorn{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Volterra, the ancient Volaterrae, Etruscan Velathri, one of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most ancient Etruscan cities, is now an episcopal residence with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}13,000 inhab., loftily situated (1602 ft.), and commanding in clear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}weather charming prospects as far as the heights of Pisa, the Apen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nines , and the sea with the islands of Gorgona, Elba, Capraja,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Corsica. The environs are dreary and desolate; the effect of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the rain on the soft and spongy soil is most prejudicial to agri¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}culture.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Volterra was one of the twelve ancient confederate cities ot{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Etruria, and was so strongly fortified that during the civil wars it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}withstood a siege by Sulla's troops for two years. It afterwards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}became a Roman municipiuni, but gradually fell to decay and was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}totally destroyed in the 10th cent. It was re-erected under the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Othos, but does not now cover one-third of its ancient area. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the middle ages it was a free town, until it became subject to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Florence in the 14th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Among the Antiquities the ancient *Town Walls, once up¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards of 4'/.j M. in circumference, and nearly three times as ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tensive as those of Fiesole and Cortona, are especially worthy of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}notice. Their dimensions (40 ft. in height, 13 ft. in thickness){% endraw %}
{% raw %}and construction of horizontal courses of sandstone blocks (pan-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}china) are best inspected outside the Porta Fiorentina and in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the garden of the monastery of Santa Chiara. One of the ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gateways, the *Porta all' Arco, 20 ft. in height, is also still in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}existence. The corbels are adorned with almost obliterated heads of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lions, or guardian deities of the city. An urn in the museum,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representing the battle of Thebes, has a similar gate upon it. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Porta di Diana ('il Portone') , another gateway, outside the Porta{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fiorentina, has been much altered. Outside the same gate, below{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the burying-ground, is situated the ancient Necropolis, about mid¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}way on the slope of the hill, at the place which is now called{% endraw %}
{% raw %}■S. Marmi. A number of the curiosities in the museum were found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here, but the tombs have all been reclosed.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Piscina, outside the castle, a reservoir resting on six co¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lumns, is only shown by permission of the bishop, and is reached{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by means of a long ladder.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Thermae, near the fountain of S. Felice, are of Roman{% endraw %}
{% raw %}origin. Traces of an Amphitheatre near the Porta Fiorentina.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Palazzo dei Priori or Palazzo Pubblico (PI. 19) in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Piazza, a handsome edifice, begun in 1208 and completed in 1257,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is unfortunately somewhat modernised; the exterior is adorned with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mediaeval coats of arms. The Museo Civico formerly kept here has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been removed to the Palazzo Tagassi (see p. 11), while its place{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has been supplied by a collection of pictures, of which the follow¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing are the most important: *Luca Signorelli, Madonna and saints,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1491; Dom. Ghirlandajo, Christ in glory (ruined by restoration{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1874), and a Madonna, by the same.{% endraw %}
