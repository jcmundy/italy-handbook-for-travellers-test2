{% raw %}Tuscan Sculptors , too, find their way frequently to Rome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and are constantly employed either as workers in bronze or marble.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Little attention seems, however, to have been paid to the former.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The great bronze doors of St. Peter, the joint work of Filarete{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Simone, are interesting rather from the wealth of mythological{% endraw %}
{% raw %}imagery with which they are embellished, than from their artistic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pretensions, which will not compare with those of Ghiberti's famous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gates. So much the more powerfully does the sculptor appeal to us{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in marble. A taste for profusion and splendour of monumental de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coration in adorning the tombs, which fact declares itself in the 15th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}century — a result probably of that thirst for fame which is identi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fied with the Renaissance — gave the sculptor unceasing opportunity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the exercise of his art, particularly in its purely decorative phases.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}There is scarcely a single church of a certain date which does not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contain sepulchral monuments from the close of the 15th century.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The church of S. Maria del Popolo possesses the largest number.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}These monuments — perfected in Florence and probably naturalised{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Rome by Mino da Fiesole — are nearly uniform, viz. a sarcopha¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gus surmounted by a statue of the deceased, and supported by a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pedestal ornamented with a garland of fruit and flowers, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}genii. A niche or panelled screen finished with a medallion of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Madonna form the usual background. The majority of these sculp¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tures cannot be traced to any particular artist. It would appear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}indeed that the sarcophagi, as with the ancient Romans, were rather{% endraw %}
{% raw %}articles of manufacture than works of art, made wholesale fashion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after some favourite pattern and bought 'ready made', a com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mission being given to the sculptor for a portrait of the deceased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to which would be added the armorial bearings with inscription.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Whoever might have visited Rome in the earlier years of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}16th century would have found himself in the presence of an in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tense movement in the art world; he would have found Archi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tect, Sculptor and Painter alike occupied with projects of more or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}less grandeur. So far, however, Rome did not in this respect sur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pass the other chief towns of Italy; so far art had not assumed that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}particular form of life and direction which only the atmosphere of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rome could sustain, or which the genius of the Vatican alone could{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quicken — during the Pontificate of Julius II. (1503—1513), where{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the golden era of Roman art began, this consummation was actu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ally achieved.{% endraw %}
