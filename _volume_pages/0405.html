{% raw %}I nder the Philosophy : 3. The so-called School of Athens (a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}name not originally applied to the work), the companion to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Disputa, not only in point of situation, but with respect to its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}subject likewise. There we are introduced to a congregation of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}believers, here to an Assembly of Scholars. The scene is not divided{% endraw %}
{% raw %}between heaven and earth, as in the case of the Disputa, but is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}confined to earth alone; while at the same time, as in the Disputa,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a gradation of knowledge, from the imperfect empirical to the per¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fect and universal, is suggested. A flight of steps leads to an open{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colonnade, crowned with a dome at the back (supposed to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been designed by Bramante), which forms the most admirable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple of knowledge ever created. Apollo, Minerva, and numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gods adorn the niches. Plato and Aristotle, the princes in the realm{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of thought, whom the Renaissance especially revered, surrounded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by a numerous train, approach the steps which descend to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}foreground, where, in contrast to the pure philosophers, is a crowd{% endraw %}

{% raw %}metic, astronomy, and music. Such are the two main contrasts{% endraw %}

{% raw %}raising of the sentiments and aspirations from mechanical pursuits.{% endraw %}
