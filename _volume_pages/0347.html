{% raw %}The Aventine.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Monte Testaccio. S. Paolo Fuori.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Aventine (151 ft.), anciently the principal seat of the RoÂ¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}man Plebs, and afterwards densely peopled, is now quite deserted,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being occupied by monasteries and vineyards only. At its base lies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Porta S. Paolo, leading to the celebrated Basilica of that name,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adjoining which is the Pyramid of Cestius with the Protestant Burial-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ground and the enigmatical Monte Testaccio. The main street skirts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the base of the hill close to the river, whilst other steep streets{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ascend the hill.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The principal street quits the Piazza Bocca della Vcrita (p. 249){% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the S. side under the name of Via della Salara. To the left,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the church of S. Maria in Cosmedin, the street mentioned at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 254 diverges to S. Prisca. About 2 min. farther, at the small{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Chapel of St. Anna, a second street diverges, leading to the three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}churches mentioned at pp. 253, 254.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The main street then runs between houses and walls of no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}interest, and under the name of Via della Marmorata (PL II, 18){% endraw %}
{% raw %}reaches the Tiber in 6 min. from the Piazza Bocca della Vcrita,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and skirts the river for about 2 min. To the right we enjoy a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pleasing retrospect of the Ponte Rotto and the Capitol. The large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}building on the opposite bank is the Hospital of S. Michele (p. 331) ;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in front of it is the small harbour where the steamers to Ostia and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Porto lie. We next reach the Marmorata (PL III, 18), the landing-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}place and depot of the unwrought marble of Carrara. In the course{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of excavations made on the bank of the river below this point since{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1867 the Emporium, or ancient quay, has been discovered.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}After following the footpath by the river for 8 min., we reach several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}raised landing-places with inclined planes to facilitate the removal of heavy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}weights. Rings for mooring vessels are still visible. Numerous blocks of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wrought and unwrought marble were found in the vicinity, some of them{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of rare quality and great value; and many still bearing the marks of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quarry, numbers, addresses, and other inscriptions.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the Marmorata the street runs between walls and through{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an archway of brick. After 6 min. the road from the three churches{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the Aventine descends from the left (see p. 254). We pass to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right through a gateway (closed with an iron gate since 1870),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where the so-called Prati del Popolo Romano begin, and follow the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}path to the left, which soon leads us to the pyramid of Cestius, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the old Protestant cemetery, and to the new cemetery beyond it.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Protestant Cemetery (PL III, 16) is open from 7 a.m. till{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dusk (custodian a few soldi). The smaller and older burying-ground{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was laid out at the beginning of the century, hut is now disused. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1825 the present burial-ground, since doubled in extent, was set{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apart for this purpose. It is a retired spot, rising gently towards{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the city-wall, affording pleasing views, and shaded by lofty cypresses,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where numerous English, American, German, Russian, and other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}visitors to Rome are interred,{% endraw %}
