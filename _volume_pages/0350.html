{% raw %}254 IV. Ancient Rome. ROME. The Aventine.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Maria Aventina, or del Priorato (PL III, 18). This church,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was founded at a very remote period, was restored by Pius V.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and altered to its present form by Piranesi in 1765.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tin the right of the entrance is an ancient sarcophagus, on which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deceased (head unfinished), surrounded by Minerva and the Muses, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}represented; the remains of a Bishop Spinelli were afterwards placed in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it. Also a statue of Piranesi, and the tombs of several members of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Maltese order (Carall'a, Caracciolo, Seripando, etc.) of the 15th cent.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the garden of the adjacent Maltese Priory, to which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church belongs, a picturesque view of the river and city is obtained.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The above named route to the Porta S. Paolo descends in 10 min.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the main road, described at p. 251 , exactly opposite the gate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}through which the route to the Protestant cemetery and the Monte{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Testaccio diverges to the right from the road.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The first street diverging from the Via della Salara (p. 251) to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the left, immediately beyond S. Maria in Cosmedin (at the bifur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cation of which we take the branch to the right), crossesthe Aventine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and again joins the main street near the Porta S. Paolo. In 10 min.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we reach S. Prisca (PL III, 21), usually closed, a church of very{% endraw %}
{% raw %}early origin, but modernised in the 17th cent. The ancient columns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have been built into the walls. It perhaps occupies the site of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple of Diana belonging to the ancient Latin League, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}founded by Servius Tullius.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Vigna Maccarani (PL III, 17), opposite the church, contains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a fragment of the venerable Servian Wall, excavated on the slope{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Aventine. (We reach it hy traversing the vineyard straight{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the end, and then taking the main path to the left.) It consists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of large blocks of tuffstone, placed alternately length and breadth¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wise. The arch here belongs to a much later period. In the latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period of the republic the wall, as the ruins indicate, was disused{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and entirely built over. Another, but more imperfect fragment may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be seen in the vigna on the other side of the street, below S. Saba.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Below S. Prisca, the street ascends, in the direction of the gate,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to S. Saba (PL III, 20), a church of great antiquity, but almost en¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tirely rebuilt in 1465. To the left in the portico is an ancient sarco¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}phagus with a representation of a wedding and Juno Pronuba. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}interior contains 14 columns, some of granite, others of marble,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with mutilated capitals ; the walls of the nave show traces of paint¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing. The church belongs to the Collegium Germanicum, and is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most easily seen on Thursday afternoons. Festival, 5th Dec.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About l'/-2 M. from the Porta S. Paolo (PL HI, 16; comp.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}map, p. 338), anciently the Porta Ostiensis, is situated the cele¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brated church of S. Paolo Fuori le Mura. About midway on the un¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}attractive route a small chapel on the left indicates the spot where,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}according to the legend, St. Peter and St. Paul took leave of each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other on their la*t journey. — Omnibus in the afternoon every{% endraw %}
