{% raw %}The N. Transept contains Scenes from the Passion, of the Sienese{% endraw %}
{% raw %}School, formerly attributed to Cavcdlini and Puccio Capanna, a pupil of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Giotto, perhaps by Pietro Lorenzelti; these paintings are in a very damag¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed condition. — To the right of the entrance to the Sacristy, in which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the treasures and the relics belonging to the church were formerly pre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}served , is a Madonna with St. Catharine and other saints (151C), by Lo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Spagna. Over the door is a portrait of St. Francis, said to have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painted by Giunta Pisano, soon after the death of the saint. — To the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left of the entrance to the church is the Pulpit, adorned with a Coro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nation of the Virgin by Simone Martini of Siena, and further on St. Francis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}receiving the stigmata , a fresco by Giotto. — The last Chapel to the N.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is dedicated to "Scenes from the life of St. Martin, by Simone Martini (be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ginning of 14th cent.); this work, though only partially preserved, still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}remains valuable as one of the ablest productions of the Sienese school,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and in many respects bears comparison with the style of Giotto and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Florentines.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The stained windows of the lower church are by Angeletto and Pietro{% endraw %}
{% raw %}da Gubbio and Bonino eVAssisi; those of the upper church are more than{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a century later.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Crypt was constructed in 1818, after the remains of St. Francis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had been discovered in a rude stone coflin. It is approached by a double{% endraw %}
{% raw %}staircase, and is lighted with candles when visited by strangers. — Behind{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the lomb stand colossal statues of Popes Pius VII. and IX.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The "Upper Church, the frescoes of which are undergoing restoration,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has recently been converted into a museum of Early Tuscan Frescoes.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}It is entered either by the principal portal, or (by applying to the sacris¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tan) from the lower church. The church is in the form of a Latin cross,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with Gothic windows. The W. side possesses a splendid rose-window and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}handsome pediments. — In the N. Transept, as we enter from the lower{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church, are remains of Scenes from the Apocalypse, by Cimabue. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}middle of the ceiling is adorned with frescoes of the four Evangelists, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the style of Cimabue and Jacopo Torriti, the mosaicist. — The S. Tran¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sept contains the remains of a large Crucifixion, Transfiguration, Cruci¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fixion of St. Peter, and Scenes from the life of Simon Magus, by Giunta{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pisano. — In the Choir: Christ in a glory, and Assumption and Death of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Virgin, both by Cimabue. — Nave. In the upper section of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. wall are sixteen scenes from the Old Testament history, from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Creation of the world to the Recognition of Joseph by his brethren; on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the N. side, sixteen scenes from the New Testament, from the Annun¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ciation to the Descent of the Holy Ghost, by pupils of Cimabue, show¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing gradual improvement in execution. The lower section contains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}twenty-eight Scenes from the life of St. Francis, illustrative of the farther{% endraw %}
{% raw %}development of the early Florentine School (Filippo Rusutli, and Gaddo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gaddi), of the close of the 13th cent. The first and the five last of these{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frescoes are certainly by the hand of Giotto. On the ceiling of the nave{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are four Angels and four Fathers of the Church , executed in the earlier{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosaic style.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Quitting the upper church and emerging on the space in front{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of it, we may follow the street ascending thence in a straight{% endraw %}
{% raw %}direction, which will lead us to the Piazza. Here is situated the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beautiful portico of a *Temple of Mimerva, with six columns of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}travertine, converted into a church of S. Maria della Minerva.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ancient inscriptions immured in the vestibule. Adjacent to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church is the entrance to the ancient Forum, which corresponded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the present Piazza, but lay considerably lower. In the forum a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Basement for a statue, with a long inscription (fee i/2 fr.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Chiesa Nuova (PI. 7), reached by descending to the right,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}near the S.E. angle of the Piazza, occupies the site of the house in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which St. Francis was born.{% endraw %}
