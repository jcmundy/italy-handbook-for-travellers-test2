{% raw %}ROMAN ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}xlv{% endraw %}
{% raw %}new style declared itself in painting which like other branches of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plastic art had more or less fallen into a state of decay meanwhile.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Architecture adapted itself to the exigencies of Christian worship,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and in allying itself to the new architectural forms, painting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acquires a new character.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The term Basilica is understood to apply to Christian temples{% endraw %}
{% raw %}up to the 10th century. The subsequent belief that a more inti¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mate relation than that suggested by a common name subsisted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}between these early Christian edifices and the forensic Basilica{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of ancient Rome, was altogether an erroneous one. The latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were in fact the Roman courts of law and places of public meet¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing. They had a place in most of the towns of the Roman em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pire and were erected in the forum, but have nothing, whether of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}origin or form, essentially in common with the early Christian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple or church. These forensic basilicas were not adapted to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}purposes of Christian worship, nor did the old Roman basilica{% endraw %}
{% raw %}serve as a model for the building of Christian places of worship.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In proof of the one assertion may be adduced the fact that the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}forensic basilicas at the ,?nd of the 4th century retained intact{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their original destination, and in individual cases have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}restored; while the other will be justified by an unprejudiced exam¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ination of the various parts of the Christian basilicas, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}give evidence of having sprung from another source than that of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the old Roman basilica. Neither did the Temple of antiquity fur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nish the model for churches built by the early Christians. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church of SS. Cosma e Damiano, of the 6th century, is the earliest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}example of a pagan temple applied to Christian use. The Chris¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tian basilica may be said rather to have grown out of the Roman{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dwelling-house, where at first the community was in the habit{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of assembling. The plan for future ecclesiastical edifices was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acquired by simply extending the proportions of the dwelling-house.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The church of S. Clemente in Rome is relatively the most perfect{% endraw %}
{% raw %}example existing of the architectural properties and internal arran¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gement of the early Christian basilica. A small portico supported{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by pillars leads to the outer court (atrium), enclosed by a colonnade{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and having in its midst a fountain (cantharus). The eastern colon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nade leads into the interior of the church which was usually divi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ded into three aisles. Two rows of columns divide the side aisles{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the loftier one in the centre known as the nave; the nave{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and aisles abut upon a half circle or apse. At right angles to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these aisles, between them and the apse, was sometimes inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}posed a third space — the transept; the altar stood within the apse{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and apart beneath a canopy supported by pillars, and in its front,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enclosed by rails or cancelli, was the choir for the officiating priests{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and two pulpits (ambones), one used for reading the Gospel, the other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Epistles. In marked contrast to the temple of antiquity, little{% endraw %}
