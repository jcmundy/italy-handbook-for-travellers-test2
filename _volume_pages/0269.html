{% raw %}III. Home on the Tiber (Left Bank).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}That part of the city which extends to the W. from the Corso as far{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as the river was uninhabited in the most ancient times (Campus Martins),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but was gradually covered with buildings as Rome extended her sway,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and as far back as the Republic, but more particularly in the reign of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Augustus, it became the site of many palatial edifices, and the new town{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of ancient Rome. This quarter, which is now densely peopled, and is in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the main mediaeval in character, consists of a network of narrow and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dirty streets and lanes, enlivened by the busy traffic of the lower classes,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and rarely intersected by great thoroughfares. Although the topography{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of these purlieus is sometimes puzzling, and their appearance uninviting,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}they contain many highly7 interesting churches and palaces, and afford the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller an excellent opportunity of becoming acquainted with mediaeval{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rome, and with the characteristics of its present inhabitants. It is pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}posed by the present government to improve this quarter by the construc¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tion of new and broad streets, but the work has not yet been begun. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}following description begins with the N. side.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}15, 14) with its prolongation, the Via della Scrofa, leads to the S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 16 min. to S. Luigi de' Francesi and the Piazza Navona.{% endraw %}
