{% raw %}The route by the Via di Porta S. Sebastiano to the gate, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ruins and buildings situated near it, are described at p. 257 et seq.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Via Appia, the military road, constructed by the censor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Appius Claudius C*cus (in B.C. 312), led hy the ancient Porta{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Capena, near the church of S. Gregorio (in the vigna of which frag¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ments of the wall of Servius were discovered in 1869), to Capua,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whence it was afterwards extended to Beneventum and Brundisium.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In 1850 it was excavated hy order of Pius IX., under the super¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intendence of Jacobini, the minister of commerce, and Canina, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}architect, as far as the 11th milestone, where it is now intersected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hy the railway to Albano. Even at the present day the Via Appia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}merits its proud ancient title of the 'queen of roads'. It affords{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perhaps the finest of all the nearer excursions in the Campagna.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Shortly after leaving the city, we enjoy a magnificent prospect, em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bracing the Campagna, the ruins of the aqueducts, and the moun¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tains, while numerous ancient tombs are situated on each side of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}road. Very few of the latter are preserved intact; but the remains of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}others have been carefully restored hy Canina, so as to convey an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}idea of their architecture and decorations. The new administration{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has unfortunately carried its system of purification somewhat too{% endraw %}
{% raw %}far, and has removed a number of inscriptions and sculptures which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}formerly lay picturesquely scattered about.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The road descends from the Porta S. Sebastiano (p. 260) by a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}declivity, which corresponds with the ancient Clivus Martis, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after 4 min. passes under the railway to Civita Vecchia. About 3{% endraw %}
{% raw %}min. farther it crosses the brook Almo, where ruins of tombs are ob¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}served on both sides. After 5 min. more the Via Ardeatina diver¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ges to the right; and on the left stands the small church of Domine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Quo Vadis, so named from the legend that St. Peter, fleeing from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the death of a martyr, here met his Master and enquired of him,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Domine quo vadis?' to which he received the reply, 'Venio iterum{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crucifigi'; whereupon the apostle, ashamed of his weakness, returned.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A copy of the footprint which Christ is said to have impressed on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marble is shown here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}By a small circular chapel, about a hundred paces beyond the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church, a field-road diverges to the left, to the Caffarella Valley{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(see p. 346). The road now ascends, being hemmed in for the next{% endraw %}
{% raw %}]/-2 M. by monotonous walls. To the right, No. 33, ll/t M. from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the gate, is the entrance to the Catacombs of St. Calixtus (p. 336),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}furnished with an inscription , and shaded with cypresses.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A little farther on the road again divides. The branch to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left is the new road which leads to S. Urbano (p. 346), the baths{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Acqua Santa (p. 348), and (21/2 M.) unites near the so-called{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ruins of Roma Vecchia (p. 345) with the high road to Albano, see{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 359.{% endraw %}
