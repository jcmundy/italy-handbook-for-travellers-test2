{% raw %}Castello S. Angelo. ROME. V. Right Bank. 277{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Moles Hadriani), after the example of the mausoleum of Augustus,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the tomb of Caecilia Metella, etc. It was completed in 140 by An¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}toninus Pius. On a substruction, 114 yds. square, now concealed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by rubbish, arose a cylinder of travertine, 80 yds. in diameter,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}encrusted with marble, of which covering no trace now remains.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Around the margin of the top of the cylinder stood numerous statues{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in marble. The cylinder was probably surmounted by another of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}smaller dimensions, on which a colossal statue of Hadrian was placed.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The head in the Sala Rotonda of the Vatican is supposed to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have belonged to this statue. According to others the pine-apple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mentioned at p. 308 formed the culminating-point of the structure.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The total height was about 165 ft. From Hadrian to Septimius{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Severus, and perhaps down to a later period, all the emperors and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their families were interred here. When the Goths under Vitiges{% endraw %}
{% raw %}besieged Rome in 537, the tomb was converted into a fortress, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the statues on the summit were hurled down on the besiegers. At{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the end of the same century, Gregory the Great, while conducting a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}procession to pray for the cessation of the plague then raging, 'beheld{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Archangel Michael sheathing his sword' above the Castello S. An¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gelo, in commemoration of which Boniface IV. erected the chapel of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}<S. Angelo inter Nubes on the summit. This was afterwards replaced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the marble statue of an angel by Montelupo, and in 1740 by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}present bronze statue by Verschaffelt. From 923 onwards the edifice{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was always used by the party in power as a stronghold for the pur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pose of maintaining their sway over the citizens. In 1379 it was al¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most entirely destroyed by theRomans. From the timeofBonifacelX.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}downwards the castle was in possession of the popes, and in 1527{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Clement VII. sustained a terrible siege here, on which occasion Ben¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}venuto Cellini asserted he had thence shot the Connetable de Bour¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bon. The outworks were constructed by Urban V., and about 1500{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the covered passage leading from the Vatican to the castle was ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ded. In 1822 the interior was freed from rubbish. The fort was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}newly fortified by Pius IX. Permission to visit it must be obtained{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the office of the commandant, Via del Burro 147, 2nd floor. A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sergeant acts as guide C/2-l &•) but more for a party). The entrance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is to the right of the sentinel's post.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The ancient entrance is seen in the court, opposite the bridge. A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}passage gradually ascended thence, winding round the walls in the in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}terior, and then diverging from them to the Tome Chamber in the centre,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which is now reached partly by other approaches. This was the last{% endraw %}
{% raw %}resting-place of Hadrian and his family, and still contains the four niches{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for the reception of the urns. These are now empty; but a sarcophagus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of porphyry, the lid of which is now used as a font in St. Peter's, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}said to have been found here. The visitor is also shown several gloomy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dungeons in which Beatrice Cenci, Cellini, Cagliostro, and others are said{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to have been incarcerated; former apartments of the popes; and a saloon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with frescoes by Raphael's pupil Perino del Vaga. The view from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}summit is remarkably fine, especially of St. Peter's. It is here that the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Girandola (p. 116) takes place.{% endraw %}
