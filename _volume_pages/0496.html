{% raw %}left to Castel Madama (see below); here we keep to the right. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arches of the venerable Aqua Marcia, and soon afterwards remains{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Aqua Claudia and the Anio Vetus become visible. About{% endraw %}
{% raw %}4 M. from the town are (1.) the ruins of the ancient Empulum. now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ampiglione, and 1 M. farther those of Sassula, beyond which a{% endraw %}

{% raw %}right to Gerano, a village with a poor osteria.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The path now ascends the heights, whence a line view of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mountains and valleys as far as Olevano (p. 372) is disclosed. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}villages to the left are Canterano and Rocca Canterano, to the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rocca S. Stefano and Civitella. After frequent ascents and descents{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the mountain-slopes, and a succession of fine views of the valleys{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the vicinity, the valley of the Anio and Subiaco below suddenly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}come into view beyond the last defile.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Carriage-Road leaves Tivoli hy the Porta S. Angelo, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}follows the right bank of the Anio. On the right, after the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mile, are seen a few arches of the Aqua Claudia, and shortly after¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards the Acqua Pia, the arches of which are frequently visible.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}After 3 M. a road diverges to the left to the lofty S. Polo, whence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Monte Gennaro may be ascended (see p. 373). Farther on is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}loftily situated village of Castello Madama (1460 ft.), which has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}long been visible; then the ruins of the old castle of Saccomuro.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About 7*/2 M. from Tivoli, we reach Vicovaro, the ancient Varia,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which possesses interesting walls of travertine-blocks, and the octa¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gonal, late-Gothic chapel of S. Giacomo (containing a miracle-work¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing image of the Madonna), designed in the 16th cent, by Simone,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a pupil of Brunellesco. Beyond Vicovaro the road divides, leading{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the left to the village of Liceuza (p. 374), and to the right hy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the river to Subiaco. Cantalupo, the Mandela of Horace (p. 374),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situated on a rock, lies on the left.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}About 172 M. from Vicovaro we pass a monastery of -S. Co-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}simato, and soon afterwards cross the Licenza, an affluent of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Anio. On the left bank of the river opens the valley of Sambuci,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}through which Siciliano and the above-described pedestrian-route{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from Tivoli to Subiaco are reached. Above the valley lies the village{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Saracinesco, which soon becomes visible. It is said to have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}founded by the Saracens. The inhabitants wear a picturesque cos¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tume. Midway between Tivoli and Subiaco stands the Osteria della{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ferrata. Farther on lies Roviano, opposite to which is Anticoli on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the left bank. Beyond Roviano the Via Valeria diverges to the left{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to Arsoli and the Lago di Fucino (see Baedeker's Southern Italy).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The valley of the Anio now expands picturesquely, and again{% endraw %}
{% raw %}contracts at Agosta. To the left lies Cervara on a lofty rock • on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right Canterano and Rocca Canterano. Subiaco, charmingly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situated amidst wood and rock, now soon becomes visible.{% endraw %}
