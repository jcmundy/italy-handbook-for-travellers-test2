{% raw %}As Fresco Painter Michael Angelo figures exclusively in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rome. Tradition tells us how loathe he was to exchange the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chisel for the brush, when at the behest of the imperious Julius II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}he undertook the decoration in fresco of the ceiling of the Sixtine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Chapel. These frescoes are nevertheless the most important of Mi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chael Angelo's contributions to art. They afford a wider field for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the exercise of his creative power than sculpture, where plastic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}forms, unequal as they are to the demands of his prolific genius, be¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tray him into exaggeration. These frescoes of Michael Angelo are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}closely akin to the wall paintings of Florentine and Umbrian artists{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the close of the 15th century, in which the deliverer of the Israelites{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is made to prefigure the Saviour of mankind. How salvation came{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the world, and how proclaimed, is the theme which Michael{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Angelo undertakes to illustrate. In the centre piece is depicted the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Creation, the history of Adam and of Noah; how sin came into the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}world, but with sin the promise of redemption. Forecasting all{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this we next see the figures of Prophets and Sibyls. In the mar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ginal pictures we see continued reference to the Redemption, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the various deliverances of the Jewish people (the brazen serpent,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}David and Goliath, the fate of Haman, and Judith), in conformity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with mediaeval conceptions, together with symbols of the Redemption.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Connecting themselves with the above are the groups occupying the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lunettes, pourtraying expectation, the anguish of suspense, and con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trition, which include at once matters of fact and a twofold allusion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the vicissitudes of the Israelites and the events of our Saviour's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}life (progenitors of Christ and Jews captive in Babylon). The sub¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}limity of the work is to be attributed very much to the skill with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which mere matters of fact are everywhere subordinated to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the claims of individual action as well as artistic purpose. Moreover{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Michael Angelo has contrived so to dispose the various portions of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his vast work, ascending by figures, single and in groups, from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}simply decorative margin to the crowning effort in the centre, so to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adapt them to the place they occupy, that the entire work becomes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}architecturally, so to speak, self-supporting; while the compo¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sition as a whole is wielded with a wealth of resources together{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a power of organisation such as no other artist has attained to.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The thoughtful beholder will not acquiesce in the exclusive study{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the central pictures. The figures in monochrome and minor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}decorations are replete with a beauty peculiar to themselves.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Of the 'Last Judgment', painted by Michael Angelo at a much{% endraw %}
{% raw %}later period(1541), it is difficult, owing to its dilapidated condition,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to form an accurate estimate. The unerring audacity, however,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with which figure ami group alike are thrown into every conceivable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}attitude and movement, must command a mute and amazed attention.{% endraw %}
