{% raw %}The island in the Tiber (Isola Tiberina, or di S. Bartolommeo){% endraw %}
{% raw %}is believed by some authorities to have been once traversed by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wooden Pons Sublicius, the most ancient communication between{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Home and its suburb on the Janiculus. It is now crossed from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Piazza Moutauara (p. 210) by the Ponte de' Quattro Capi (PL II,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}17), so named from the four-headed figures on the balustrades,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}constructed in B.C. 62 by L. Fabricius, as the inscription records.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}On the island, to the right, is the church ol'S. Giovanni Colabita{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL II, 17), which, with the neighbouring monastery and hospital,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}belongs to the Brothers of Charity, hy whom sick strangers are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}readily received. Farther on, to the left, is a small piazza, embel¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lished in 1869 with a monument to SS. John, Francis, Bartho¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lomew, and Paulinus. Here, perhaps on the site of an ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple of ^Esculapius, is situated the church of —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Bartolommeo (PL II, 18), erected about the year 1000 by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Emp. Otho III. in honour of St. Adalbert of Gnesen, and er¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}roneously named S. Bartolommeo. The emperor had desired the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Heneventans to send him the relics of St. Bartholomew, but received{% endraw %}
{% raw %}those of St. Paulinus of Nola in their stead. The present church,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the exception of the campanile, is modernised and uninterest¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing; facade by Lunghi, 1625.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Interior contains fourteen ancient columns; in tlie choir,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}remains of an early mosaic. In the centre of the steps teading to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}presbyterium is the mouth of a fountain of the 12th cent., on which a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figure of Christ with a book in his hand, and the heads of two side-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ligures are alone distinguishable.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the small Garden op the Monastery (visitors ring at the entrance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the right by the church) is seen part of the ancient bulwark of travertine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which gave the island the appearance of a ship. An obelisk represented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the mast. The figure of a snake hewn on the bow of the ship is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reminiscence of the story that the Romans, when sorely afflicted by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plague, sent for /Esculapius from Epidaurus in B.C. 293, and that a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}snake, a reptile sacred to the god, concealed itself in the vessel, and on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reaching the harbour escaped to this island, which was dedicated to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}/Esculapius in consequence. That the god was worshipped here has been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}proved by the discovery in the island of limbs in terracotta, which were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}presented by sick persons as votive offerings.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The island is connected with Trastevere by the ancient Pons{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cestius (Gratianus), now the Ponte S. Bartolommeo (PL II, 18),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was built by Augustus, and, according to the lengthy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inscription on the light side, restored by the Emperors Valentinian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Gratian. Pleasant view to the right. The wooden mills in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}river, in the direction of the Ponte Sisto, date from the siege of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Belisarius, when the Goths destroyed the aqueducts, thus rendering{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the mills on the Janiculus useless.{% endraw %}
