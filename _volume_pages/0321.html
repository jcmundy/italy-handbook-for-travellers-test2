{% raw %}On the E. side of the Forum, with its front towards the Capitol,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is situated the Temple of Caesar, to which Caesar, in addition to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other alterations made by him, transferred the tribune of the orators.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This was now named the Rostra Julia, and from it, on the occasion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the funeral of the murdered dictator on the 19th or 20th March,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}B.C. 44, Mark Antony pronounced the celebrated oration which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wrought so powerfully on the passions of the excited populace. A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}funeral pyre was hastily improvised, and the unparalleled honour{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accorded to the illustrious dead of being burned in view of the most{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sacred shrines of the city. A column with the inscription 'parenti{% endraw %}
{% raw %}patriae' was afterwards erected here to commemorate the event. At{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a later period Augustus erected this temple in honour of'Divus Ju¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lius', his deified uncle and adoptive father, and dedicated it to him{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in B.C. 29, after the battle of Actium. At the same time he adorned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Rostra with the prows of the captured Egyptian vessels.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The foundation of the substructions of the temple, consisting of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}concrete, were discovered in 1872, but their covering of solid stone has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been removed. In front of the temple there are the remains of a plat¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}form, still partly paved with slabs of stone, which is believed to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been the rostra of imperial Rome. Its present form appears to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}resulted from subsequent alterations.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Between the temple of Caesar and that of Faustina situated to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the E. of it are several ruins of late Roman and early mediaeval{% endraw %}
{% raw %}buildings with remains of a pavement in marble mosaic. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}temple of Faustina is now connected with the excavated part of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Forum, a cutting having been made through the street which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}passes it, and the rubbish having been cleared away. In front of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the temple runs an ancient street, the Via Sacra (comp. p. 225).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the ruts of wheels still visible. From this street the temple{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is approached by a flight of steps interrupted in the middle by a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}projecting platform.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The * Temple of Faustina, of which the portico (with ten co¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lumns, six of which form the facade) and part of the cella are still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}standing, was dedicated by Antoninus in 141 to his wife, the elder{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Faustina, and re-dedicated to that emperor himself after his death.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The first line of the inscription, Divo Antonino et divae Faustinae{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ex 8.C., was then added. In the interior of the temple is the church{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of S. Lorenzo in Miranda.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The portico was excavated in 1807 and 1810. (In front, of it once{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stood the Arcus Fabianus, erected in honour of Fabius Maximus, the con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}queror of the Allobrogi, in A.D. 123.) The columns are of cipollino, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marble of Eubcea, and are 46 ft. in height. The cella is of peperine, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marble incrustation of which has entirely disappeared. — The year of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}foundation of the church is unknown, and the earliest record of it dates{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from 1430. The facade was erected in 1602. The entrance is at present{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the Via di S. Lorenzo in Miranda.{% endraw %}
