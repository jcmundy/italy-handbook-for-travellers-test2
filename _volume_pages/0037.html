{% raw %}ANCIENT ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}XXXI{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and, let it be borne in mind, the architecture of the Greeks did not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}start into being as Athena from the head of Jupiter, fully armed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and equipped. Careful observation will detect in the Orders associ¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ated in name with the two principal Hellenic races evidences of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}very gradual development. Subsequently, to these two orders was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}added a third, known as the Corinthian. It was inevitable that in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the practice of Sculpture as well as Architecture divergences arose{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which may be characterised as provincialisms; and it is equally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intelligible that as Art in Greece assumed more and more majes¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tic proportions, these provincialisms should have become less and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}less observable.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}They were finally obliterated by the glories of what may be distin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}guished , in reference to art, as the Age of Pericles; glories{% endraw %}
{% raw %}associated with the talismanic names of Phidias and Polycletus in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sculpture, and of Ictinus and Mnesicles in Architecture. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}golden age of the Italian Renaissance, when Raphael filled the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}world with rapturous wonder, can alone be compared to a time{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which witnessed the surpassing achievements of art in Greece. Of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the painters of this period, of Polygnotus for example, who flourished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}somewhat earlier, little can be ascertained. Their works have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perished ; and all that we can learn of them is at best too hypothe¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tical to be worthy of record.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The name of Polycletus belongs to the Peloponnesus. The con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}noisseur of his day claimed for him the merit of having presented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the human form complete in its minutest details, correct in pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portion, perfect in symmetry. One of his works in particular, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figure of a powerful youth armed with a spear (Doryphorus).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was upheld as an example of the master's peculiar excellence.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and hence was surnamed the Kanon. As a counterpart to the Dory-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}phorus, and in like manner regarded as a type or model, is the figure{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a youth of distinguished beauty, who with both hands folds a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}band round his head (Diadumenus). Of one of his Amazons it was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}said, that it surpassed even the Amazon of Phidias in beauty.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Finally, especial glory was claimed for his statue of Hera placed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in a temple dedicated to this goddess at Delphi. — Myron s chief{% endraw %}
{% raw %}delight was to pourtray the human form in action, but his success{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was, to say the least of it, partial. Thus he represents his Disco¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bolus actually doubled up in the act of throwing the discus. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a group on the Acropolis at Athens the same artist has chosen for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his subject the incident of Marsyas in the act of seizing the pipes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which the Goddess Athena has rejected, and on which he plays{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while dancing with delight. He recoils in terror and bewilderment{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as theGoddess, suddenly appearing, dashes the pipes from his hand.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Finally the Cow of Myron was especially popular. It was boasted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of her that so life-like was she that she deceived both man and beast:{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nay imposed even on a living calf. But mightiest amidst this{% endraw %}
