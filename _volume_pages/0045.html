{% raw %}ANCIENT ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}XXXIX{% endraw %}
{% raw %}' dias. Numbers of statues belonging to the Periclean age have come{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to light in Rome, replicas for the most part of Victors in the Games{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and of Amazons. Figures of the Gods, with few exceptions, belong{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to a later period. The most numerous, and also very charming, were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the graceful forms of more recent Attic art, represented by Praxiteles{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and his compeers; also the elegant and animated creations of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lysippian and post-Lysippian schools. It is hardly conceivable that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the museums of Rome, filled as they are with works of sculpture{% endraw %}
{% raw %}collected on the spot, no original works are to be found. Assuredly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there has been a time when they have not been wanting in Rome:{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and it seems improbable that one and all should have been lost{% endraw %}
{% raw %}iu the devastation which has more than once made wreck of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Eternal City. Certain it is, however, that the greater part of what{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we now see are either replicas or copies. This fact is determined{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the material. The great statues of Phidias and Praxiteles set{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apart for the temples were of gold and ivory, while Polycletus and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lysippus worked in bronze. In Attica, too, this costly material was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preferred by the earlier sculptors. It was only by degrees that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marble came into use for groups as well as single figures. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}'Discus thrower' of Myron, as well as the groups of Marsyas{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Athena were originally in bronze. In the Palazzo Massimi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}alle Colonne there is to be seen a striking figure readily recogni¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sed as that of Myron's 'Discus thrower', but it is in marble. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}like manner the Marsyas in the Lateran is of marble, and so also is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Apoxyomenus in the Vatican museum. Just as we moderns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}delight in the copy or engraving of some celebrated picture, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}amateur of old gave his commission for the copy of some favourite{% endraw %}
{% raw %}statue, to be executed in bronze, or, more frequently, in marble.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At any rate comparatively few works in bronze, of importance in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}point of size, are preserved. It was not enough to have simply{% endraw %}
{% raw %}repeated the celebrities of sculpture. The artists of the so-called{% endraw %}
{% raw %}New Attic School, which flourished in the last centuryB. C, wefind{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reproducing the works of their predecessors very effectively with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}such departures from the original as are to be seen in a more arti¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ficial and highly wrought arrangement of drapery, a more decorative{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rendering of the detail generally, and an attempt to impart increased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}animation to the figure as a whole. Such piracies, when tolerably{% endraw %}
{% raw %}successful, became in their turn models for numerous imitations.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The results of this process are exactly what the connoisseur will{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be prepared to find , and such as present themselves in the collec¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tions of antique art in Rome. He must not expect to find himself in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the presence of the simplicity and directness as well as grandeur of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aim characteristic of Greek art in her loftiest moods : but rather of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}her attenuation in the shape of imitations and adaptations, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}growth of the Imperial age. Antique art, however, exhibits through¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}out its career an astonishing vitality and continuity. The spirit of{% endraw %}
