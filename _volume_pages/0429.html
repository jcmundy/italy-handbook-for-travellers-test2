{% raw %}This small palace is one of the most pleasing Renaissance edifices in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rome, being simple in style, and of symmetrical proportions. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceiling of the principal chamber on the ground floor, which is entered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}direct from the garden, was designed by Raphael (1518-20), and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}decorated by Giulio Romano, Francesco Penni, and others of his pu¬{% endraw %}

{% raw %}among the most charming creations of the master (comp. p. Lvii).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The hall was originally open, but is now furnished with large win¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dows in order to protect the pictures.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}is continued on the wall opposite the entrance. Raphael adhered to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}charming fable of Apuleius, which may be briefly told as follows. A certain{% endraw %}
{% raw %}king had three daughters, of whom Psyche, the youngest, excites the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}jealousy of Venus by her beauty. The goddess accordingly directs her son{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cupid to punish the princess by inspiring her with love for an unworthy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}individual (1). Cupid himself becomes enamoured of her, shows her to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Graces (2), and carries her off (this is the best preserved of the paintings).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}He visits her by night only, warning her not to indulge in curiosity as to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his appearance. Psyche, however, instigated by her envious sisters, dis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obeys the injunction. She lights a lamp, a drop of heated oil from which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}awakens her sleeping lover. Cupid upbraids her, and quits her in anger.{% endraw %}
