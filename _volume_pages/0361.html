{% raw %}As already mentioned at p. 263, and as the annexed plan and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sections show, there exist below the present church several still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}older strata of masonry. Lowest of all, and forming a right angle,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are two massive walls, constructed of blocks of stone quarried on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Csclius itself (No. 1. in the ground-plan and in the section).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}As the stones are more carefully hewn and jointed than those of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Servian wall, these substructions cannot be dated farther back than{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the republican epoch. Above these are remains of workmanship{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the imperial era, executed in the 2nd cent, after Christ (No. II.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the ground-plan and in the section; see also p. 266). Upon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these foundations in the 4th cent, was erected the Christian basilica{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which now forms the Lower Church (No. III. in the ground-plan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and in the section), the altar of which stood at the point marked a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the section. This seems to have been a much grander edifice{% endraw %}
{% raw %}than the church afterwards superimposed, its nave having been as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}broad as that of the upper church and one of its aisles put together{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(see ground-plan), and the lower apse was accordingly of wider{% endraw %}
{% raw %}span than the upper. During the construction of the upper church{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the lower was entirely covered up , and the two churches were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}never in use at the same time. It is only since about the year{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1855 that the lower has been again rendered accessible, and it is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now shown by the sacristan, who provides a light (fee 1 fr.). In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}order, however, to obtain a distinct idea of the original structure,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which has been considerably marred by alterations, the visitor should{% endraw %}
{% raw %}repair to the church on 23rd Nov., 1st Feb., or on the second Mon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}day in Lent, on which days the lower church is completely illu¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}minated. The entrance is from the sacristy of the upper church (in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right aisle), on the walls of which are hung copies of the fres¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}coes in the lower church, and plans comparing the upper with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lower part of the edifice.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A broad marble staircase, with inscriptions on the walls from the time{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Pope Damasus, descends to the Vestibule in which the nave and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}aisles of the lower church terminate. The aisles alone have remained in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their original condition, while in the nave additions of three distinct{% endraw %}
{% raw %}periods are observable. The newest are the buttresses constructed during{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the recent excavations for the support of the upper church, and recognisable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by their whitewash. The older additions consist of the wall between the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns of the right aisle, and the lateral wall on the right, both built on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the occasion of the erection of the upper church, the former for the sup¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}port of the external wall above, the latter to sustain the right row of{% endraw %}
