{% raw %}The Piazza S. Carlo is next reached. Here, on the right, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Carlo al Corso (PL I, 17), the national church of the Lombards,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the resort of the fashionable world, with a tasteless facade. It{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was erected in the 17th cent, by the two Lunyhi and Pietro da{% endraw %}

{% raw %}mending S. Carlo Borromeo to Christ (the heart of the saint is preserved{% endraw %}
{% raw %}under the altar). The chief festival of the church is celebrated on 4th Nov.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the opposite side, the Via Carrozza, and farther on the Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}de Condotti diverge to the left to the Piazza di Spagna (p. 144);{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while the prolongation of the latter to the right, the Via della Fon-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tanella di Borghese, leads to the Palazzo Borghese (p. 188) and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the bridge of S. Angelo (p. 276).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Farther on in the Corso, on the right, No. 418 A, is the spacious{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Palazzo Ruspoli (PL I, 16), built in 1586 by Ammanati, and now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}containing the Banca Nazionale.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the left the Via Borgognona and Via Frattina diverge to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Piazza di Spagna. In the small piazza which lies opposite the en¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}trance of the Via Frattina, to the right of the Corso, rises on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left S. Lorenzo in Lucina (PI. I, 16), a church of very ancient{% endraw %}
{% raw %}origin, but frequently restored. The campanile, the upper part of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which is modern, is now the only old part of the building. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church, with the adjoining monastery, has since 1606 belonged to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Minorites, who have given it its present form.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Portico is supported by four columns; at the door are two half-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}immured mediaeval lions. — In the Interior, by the 2nd pillar to the{% endraw %}
