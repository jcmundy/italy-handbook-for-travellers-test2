{% raw %}At the entrance to the upper part of the town, on the site of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}citadel, which was removed in 1860, extends the Piazza Vittomo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Emanuele (PI. B, 5), in which rises the Prefettura, a simple and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}handsome modern building, adorned with arcades on the ground-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}floor. The garden terrace affords a *superb view of the Umbrian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}valley with Assisi, Spello, Foligno, Trevi, and numerous other vil¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lages , enclosed by the principal chain of the Apennines extending{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from Gubbio onwards ; the Tiber and part of the lower quarters of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Perugia are also visible. (A band plays here twice a week.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}Northwards from the Piazza Vittorio Emanuele runs the Corso{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the left, leading to the Cathedral square ; and the Via Riaria to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the right, leading to the Piazza Sopramura (p. 53).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We follow the Corso to the left, which is the busiest and hand¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}somest street in the town. No. 241, on the right, is the Palazzo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Baldeschi (PL 21 ; B, 4), on the 2nd floor of which is preserved a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*drawing by Raphael (PinturicchioV) for the 5th fresco in the library{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the cathedral of Siena (p. 28; fee i/2 fr.).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the left, farther on, is No. 249, the *Collegio del Cambio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL 31; B, 4), the old chamber of commerce, containing frescoes by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Perugino, dating from his best period, 1500. (Custodian i/2 fr.; best{% endraw %}
{% raw %}light in the morning.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}These frescoes adorn the -Sala del Cambio: on the right, Sibyls and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Prophets ; above, God the Father; on the left, heroes, kings , and philo¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sophers of antiquity; opposite, the Nativity and Transfiguration; on a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pillar to the left, the portrait of Perugino; the whole surrounded by-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}admirable arabesques. Raphael is said to have been one of Perugino's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pupils who assisted in the execution of these frescoes, and whose handi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}work is traceable in the arabesques on the ceiling. Perugino received{% endraw %}
{% raw %}350 ducats for his work from the guild of merchants. The carved and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inlaid work ('tarsia') of the judicial benches, doors, etc., by Antonio Mer-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}catello, which are amongst the finest Renaissance works of the kind,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also deserve notice. — The adjacent Chapel contains an altar-piece and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frescoes by Giannicola Manni.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Immediately adjoining the Collegio is the *Palazzo Pubblico{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(or Comunale, PL 22; B, 4), a huge edifice of 1281 and 1333, re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cently skilfully restored, with its principal facade towards the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Corso and a second towards the Piazza del Duomo. It is adorned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with fine windows, a handsome portal, and Gothic sculptures (the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}armorial bearings of the allied town, saints, etc.). In the group of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}animals over the chief entrance, the griffin represents Perugia, while{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the wolf, overcome by it, is Siena. The victory gained by the Peru-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gians in 1358 over the Sienese is also commemorated by trophies{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(chains, bars of gates) on the portal in the Piazza del Duomo. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Sala della Prefettura (third story, entrance from the Corso) are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}damaged frescoes from the history of St. Herculanus and St. Louis of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Toulouse by Bonfigli.{% endraw %}
