{% raw %}ROMAN ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}liii{% endraw %}
{% raw %}With the names of Bramante and Michael Angelo is associated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that of Raphael (1483-1520), whose youthful genius had very{% endraw %}
{% raw %}early declared itself, first in Perugia and later in Florence. In Rome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are to be seen interesting mementoes of both these periods. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Coronation of the Virgin in the Vatican Gallery we see him still in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the trammels of the Umbrian School; the effects of his Florentine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}training are visible in his Entombment of Christ in the Borghese{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gallery (belonging to later periods are the so called Fornarina in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Barberini Gallery, the Violin Player in the Palazzo Sciarra,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Navagero and Beazzano in the Doria Gallery, the Madonna di Fo¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ligno, and the Transfiguration, the master's last work, both in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Vatican Gallery). The majority of Raphael's easel pictures are to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be found elsewhere than in Rome.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}But in Rome only could Raphael have found a field suited to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the exercise of his highest powers in Fresco Painting. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mural paintings in the state apartments of the Popes in the Vatican{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palace must first be noticed. In order rightly to appreciate these,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it must not on the one hand be forgotten that fresco painting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}never completely loses its decorative character; nor on the other{% endraw %}
{% raw %}must the peculiar position of the Pontificate in the beginning of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 16th century be lost sight of. In the palace of the Vatican the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}same courtly tone, the same pursuit of sensuous pleasures, of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mere joys of existence , prevailed as in the courts of the younger{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Italian dynasties; expressions of national sentiment met with a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}favorable reception , while an active agitation on the part of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Humanists did not appear to have compromised the dignity of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Papal Court. These conditions are more or less distinctly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reflected in the frescoes of Raphael. The courtier repeatedly asserts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}himself; even a delicate compliment to the patron is not disdained,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nor the ceremonial spectacle excluded. Political as well as personal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}allusions are not wanting, while ample space is devoted to the glo¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rification of the Humanistic ideal. Finally, when it is borne in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mind that Raphael was constantly compelled to defer to the exigen¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cies of the allotted space, to study the separate requirements of wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and ceiling, we gain an insight into the nature and extent of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}restraints imposed upon the Artist. They beset him indeed on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}every hand, and constantly compel him to alter or modify his design.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Curiously enough these restrictions are to this day interpreted as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an act of the Artist's free and daring will. One wonders at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}amount of theological learning, of philosophical erudition displayed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the Disputa and the School of Athens, as well as at the inventive¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ness which could connect subjects so remote from one another as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Heliodorus driven from the Temple, and the expulsion of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}French from Italy. Through the entire range of subjects there{% endraw %}
{% raw %}runs a vein of profound and continuous thought. But especially{% endraw %}
{% raw %}admirable are alike the discernment which enabled Raphael to{% endraw %}
