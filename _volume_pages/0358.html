{% raw %}262 IV. Ancient Rome. ROME.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Caelius.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so called from the small marble ship which Leo X. caused to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}made from the model of the ancient original formerly in the portico{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the church.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The church of S. Maria in Domnica, or della Navicella, one of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the most ancient deaconries of Rome, was re-erected by Paschalis I.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 817, to which period the columns of the nave and the tribune{% endraw %}
{% raw %}belong; the portico, erected by Leo X., is said to have been de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}signed by Raphael.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Interior. The Nave rcsls on eighteen beautiful columns of granite;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above, below the ceiling, is a frieze painted by Giulio Romano and Perino{% endraw %}
{% raw %}del Vaga (in grisaille; genii and lions in arabesques), afterwards retouched.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The arch of the Tribune rests on two columns of porphyry; the mosaics{% endraw %}
{% raw %}date from the 9th cent., but were considerably restored under Clement XL;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above the arch, Christ between two angels and the apostles, below are two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}saints; in the vaulting, the Madonna and Child imparting blessings, on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cither side angels, Paschalis I. kissing her foot; beneath all the figures{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spring forth flowers. — The church is open on the 2nd Sunday of Lent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Opposite this church , but not accessible from the Piazza della{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Navicella, rises S. Stefano Rotondo. We follow the Via di S. Ste-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fano to the left, pass through the first green door on the right, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ring a bell to the right under the porch.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Stefano Rotondo (PL III, 27) is a very interesting build¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing, on account of its construction, and, although greatly diminished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in extent, is the largest circular church in existence. It was erected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the close of the 5th cent, by Simplicius, and afterwards gorgeous¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ly decorated with marble and mosaics. It then fell to decay, hut was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}restored hy Nicholas V. In the original edifice, the diameter of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was 70 yds., the present external wall formed the central{% endraw %}
{% raw %}row of columns, while another lower wall, decorated with pilasters,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}11 yds. distant, and still traceable round the church, formed the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}circumference. The church thus consisted of three concentric rings,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intersected by two transepts. Nicholas V- shut out the external{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wall, and filled up the spaces between the central columns with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}masonry, with the exception of a few projecting chapels. The roof{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is rudely constructed of wood. The old entrance was on the E.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side. In the present portico, erected by Nicholas, on the right, is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ancient episcopal throne, from which Gregory the Great de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}livered one of his homilies. Festival, 26th Dec.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Interior. To the left of the entrance is an altar-niche with mosaic of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 7th cent.; farther on, to the left, a chapel with (1.) a well-executed mon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ument of the beginning of the 16th cent. Most of the fifty-six columns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are of granite, a few of marble. The lateral walls bear frescoes of fearful{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scenes of martyrdom, by Tempesta and Pomarancio (much retouched). In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the centre a canopy of wood. The dome is borne by two lofty columns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of granite and two buttresses.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beyond the church the Via di S. Stefano leads past the exten¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sive fragments of an ancient aqueduct in 5 min. to the vicinity of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Lateran (p. 267).{% endraw %}
