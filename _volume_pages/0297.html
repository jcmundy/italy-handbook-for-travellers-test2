{% raw %}dered by the Vandals and robbed of its gilded bronze fifes. After that{% endraw %}

{% raw %}For nearly 500 years after the time of Cassiodorus, the Goth (6th cent.),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there is no mention of the Capitol in the annals of Rome. The hill was in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the possession of the monastery of Aracceli, and the name of Monte Caprino,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or hill of goats, which was applied to the S.E. height, bears testimony{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to its desertion. The glorious traditions, however, which attached to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this spot, gave rise to a renewal of its importance on the revival of a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spirit of municipal independence at Rome. In the 11th century it again{% endraw %}
{% raw %}became the centre of the civic administration. The prefect of the city{% endraw %}
{% raw %}resided here; among the ruins of the venerable citadel the nobility and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the citizens held their public assemblies; and in 1341 Petrarch was crown¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed as a poet in the great senate-hall here. The hill could originally be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}approached from the Forum only, the N.W. side being precipitous and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inaccessible, but in 1348 the latter side was connected for the first time{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with the new quarter of the city by the construction of the flight of steps{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Aracceli, which was almost the only public work executed at Rome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}during the exile of the papal court at Avignon. About 1389 Boniface IX.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}converted the palace of the senate into a kind of fortress, but its present{% endraw %}
{% raw %}form dates from the 16th century. Two new approaches from the city{% endraw %}
{% raw %}having been constructed in 1536, the Capitol has since formed a kind of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}termination of the modern part of the city in the direction of the ruins{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of ancient Rome.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the Piazza Aracosli (PL II, 17) three approaches lead{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Capitoline Hill, that in the centre being the principal as¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cent for pedestrians. On the left a lofty flight of 124 steps, con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}structed in 1348, leads to the church of S. Maria in Aracali (the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}principal entrance, but generally closed, see below). —On the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Via delle Tre Pile, which has recently been converted into an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}easy and handsome approach, on which occasion remains of the an¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cient wall of Servius, enclosing the hill in the direction of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Campus Martius, were brought to light (behind the railings to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left as we ascend), leads past the entrance of the Pal. Caffarelli,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was erected in the 16th cent, by Ascanio Caffarelli, a former{% endraw %}
{% raw %}page of Charles V., and is now the residence of the German am¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bassador. The principal approach and the Via delle Tre Pile lead to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Piazza del Campidoglio, see p. 213.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*S. Maria in Aracceli (PL II, 20), a church of very early origin,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is mentioned in history in the 9th cent, as -S'. Maria de Capitolio.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The present name, derived from a well-known legend (p. 212),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has been in use since the 14th century. The church, of which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman senate formerly enjoyed the patronage, has given a title to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a cardinal since the time of LeoX. The facade is unfinished, having{% endraw %}
{% raw %}escaped modernisation owing to the timely remonstrances of t,he{% endraw %}
{% raw %}celebrated German artist Overbeck.{% endraw %}
