{% raw %}RIMINI.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}11. Route. 85{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rimini, beautifully situated on the Adriatic at the mouth of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ausa and Marecchia, with 10,000 inhab. (incl. surrounding villages{% endraw %}
{% raw %}34,000), and extensive fisheries and silk manufactories, has of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}late come into notice as a sea-bathing place. Handsome public rooms{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a cafe" and restaurant, and numerous lodging-houses have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected on the pleasant promenade along the sea.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rimini, the ancient Ariminum, a town of the Umbrians, became a Ro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}man colony in B. C. 269, and formed the frontier fortress of Italy in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}direction of Gaul, and the termination of the Via Ftaminia from Rome,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was constructed in 22(1, and afterwards extended and embellished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Julius Csesar and Augustus. During the Exarchate, Rimini was the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most northern of the Pentapolis Maritime/, or 'Five Maritime Cities', which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were under the jurisdiction of one president. The other four were Pesaro,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Fano, Siuigaglia, and Ancona. In 260, Ariminum became an episcopal see,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and in 350 a council against Arianism was held here. The town after¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wards belonged to the Lombards. In 1200 it was given by Otho IV. to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Malatestas, who were at first vicegerents of the emperor, but subse¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quently hereditary princes. In 1503 they surrendered the town to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Venetians, from whom it was finally wrested by the Pope. Insurrections{% endraw %}
{% raw %}broke out here in 1845 and 1853. — It was from the history of the Mala¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}testas that Dante derived the episode of 'Francesca da Rimini' in the 5th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}canto of the Inferno.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A broad road leads from the station to the Porta Marina, beyond{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which it is called the Via Principe Umberto. After about 4 min. the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Via al Tempio Malatestiano diverges to the left, and leads to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}principal church of —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}*S. Francesco {Duomo, Tempio dei Malatesta; PI. 3), built in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 14th cent, in the Italian Gothic style, but magnificently remodell¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed in 1447-50 by Sigismundo Malatesla from designs by Leo Bat-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tista Alberti in the early Renaissance style. It is one of the principal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}works of the master, but unfortunately the lower part only of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}facade has been completed. On the coping round the church are the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arms of the Malatesta and other families allied with them (the ele¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}phant and rose being the armorial bearings of Sigismundo and his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wife Isotta). The seven vaults on the S. side contain sarcophagi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the poets, orators, philosophers and warriors whom Sigismundo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Malatesta (d. 1468), the illustrious enemy of Pope Pius II., enter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tained at his court.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Interior, which is destitute of aisles, has an open roof and a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}series of spacious lateral chapels. The massive pilasters with their rich{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ornamentations were designed by Alberti. To the right of the entrance is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the monument of Sigismund. Between the first and second chapel on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right is the entrance of the Chapel of Relics (Santuario, shown by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sacristan), containing a "fresco by Piero della Francesco CPetri de Burgo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}opus 145V), representing Sigismund Malatesta kneeling before his patron{% endraw %}
{% raw %}St. Sigismund, king of Hungary. In the Cappella di S. Michele, the 2nd{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the right, is the tomb of Isotta (d. 1450), the wife of Sigismund. — The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}first chapel to the left, restored in 1868, was destined by Sigismund Mala¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}testa for the reception of his ancestors and descendants, as the inscription{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the sarcophagus on the left testifies.{% endraw %}
