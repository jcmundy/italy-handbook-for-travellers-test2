{% raw %}Short{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pussino, named after a painter of that name, with a picturesquely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situated 'tenuta'. On the right of the road are the ruins of an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ancient tomb, named Tor di Quinto.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Within 3 M. from the Ponte Molle the Via Flaminia crosses the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Valchetta, a brook descending from Veii, the ancient Cremera,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where the Fabians sustained their well-known defeat. Beyond the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tiber lies Castel Giubileo, the ancient Fidena; (p. 350). About{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1 M. farther the road reaches the Casale di Prima Porta, with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the ruins of the imperial Villa ofLivia, or Ad Gallinas, in which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the statue of Divus Augustus (in the Vatican) was excavated in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1863. Since then the work of excavation has been continued. A{% endraw %}
{% raw %}room with * mural paintings, representing a garden with trees,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plants, and birds, all in excellent preservation, is particularly inter¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}esting (y2 fr.). — Not far from Prima Porta is a station of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ancient road, called Saxa Rubra; here, in the plain hy the river,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Maxentius was defeated in 312.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The road then leads by Rignano (15y2 M-; P- 63) to Civita{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Castellana (23 M. from Rome; p. 63).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the Porta Angelica (PL I, 8).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Two principal routes issue from the Porta Angelica (to the N.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Borgo, near the Vatican) : that in a straight direction to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ponte Molle; see p. 351. — The road to the left leads to Monte{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mario, the N. eminence of the range of hills which forms the Jani¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}culus. It was anciently named Clivus Cinnae, in the middle ages{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Monte Malo, and its present name is derived from Mario Mellini,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the proprietor of the villa mentioned below in the time of Sixtus IV.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}After passing several osterie, which are favourite resorts of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lower classes in October , we reach the foot of the hill, 1 M. from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the gate. The yellow building on the cypress-planted hill to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right is the Villa Mellini. The white building before us is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tivoli, a new place of recreation, with cafe and restaurant, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walkers may reach in !/4 hr. by a path to the left ascending in steps{% endraw %}
{% raw %}('ingresso pei pedoni'); the carriage road diverges farther on. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}terrace of the building commands an extensive , and almost unob¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}structed * View of Rome, the Campagna, the mountains , and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plain stretching down to the sea.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The carriage-road then ascends in long windings, which may be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cut off by means of steep footpaths. About 3/4 M. from the lower,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and '/4 M. from the upper entrance to the Tivoli, we pass the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}church of S. Maria del Rosario on the left, and beyond it to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right the chapel of S. Croce di M. Mario.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}We next, by a pine-tree, reach the entrance to the Villa Mellini{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(adm. !/2 fr. each person). Traversing an avenue of evergreen oaks{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we arrive at the avenue passing the villa and running along the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}brow of the hill to its culminating point (476 ft.). Here also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the view is unbounded on every side. Near the villa is an *Osteria{% endraw %}
{% raw %}commanding a beautiful view.{% endraw %}
