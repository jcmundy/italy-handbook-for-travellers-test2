{% raw %}where Greek influence had long been active, considerable proficiency{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as well as activity prevailed in the pursuit of art, although but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}scanty traces of the purity and elevation of Greek |taste are dis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cernible. In Rome, however, the Greeks of Southern Italy grafted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their art on that of the Etruscans. A bronze toilet casket (the so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}called Ficoronian Cista) found in Palestrina, which was executed in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the workshop of Novius Plautius in the 3rd century B.C., exhibits{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in its tracery a purity of design unmistakably Greek, although{% endraw %}
{% raw %}differing little in shape and plastic accessories from the very ordin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ary and often rude vessels of the period. The Romans highly esteem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed faithful Portraits. Likenesses of ancestors were preserved in wax{% endraw %}
{% raw %}masks, and displayed on occasions of ceremony. The plastic, art of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Etruscans gives evidence of a certain grasp of portraiture, which,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}though not profound, was still effective. As Roman rule extended{% endraw %}
{% raw %}itself over Southern Italy and Sicily, and later to Greece and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Asiatic continent, a constantly increasing number of works by Greek{% endraw %}
{% raw %}artists found their way to Rome, for the most part, probably, as spoil{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of war. Presently, too, the wealthy patrician, following the bent of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his individual taste or the prevailing fashion, gave commissions for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}works to be executed in Greece, or in Rome itself, to which ne¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cessity had brought many artists. Rome thus became the recognised{% endraw %}
{% raw %}centre of a taste and fashion which she could call her own. Both{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stood in close relation, not only in point of time, but in a community{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of idea and aspiration, to the art of a period immediately following{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the reign of Alexander. There is no doubt, however, that a vast{% endraw %}
{% raw %}number of works are accepted as specifically Roman only because all{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traces of the Greek models have been lost. From these, it may be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}taken for granted, the artists of Imperial Rome derived the designs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or at least the suggestion of works of utility, as well as of buildings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}devoted to mere display, such as temples. palaces, triumphal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}arches, and tombs.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}the Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian, as they are to be seen in Athens,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and comparing these examples with their reproductions in Rome.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}will have little difficulty in detecting a divergence which cannot{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be explained by differences either in forms of worship or in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}general scope of the design (a conspicuous basement with flights of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}steps in front). The delicate modelling of the best period of Greek{% endraw %}
{% raw %}art was in Roman hands either simplified, and so denuded of its true{% endraw %}
{% raw %}artistic significance — looking in short like the work of the handi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}craftsman — or so overloaded as to become hopelessly confused.{% endraw %}
