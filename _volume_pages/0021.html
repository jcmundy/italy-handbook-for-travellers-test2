{% raw %}No month in the year can be pronounced absolutely unfavourable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for travelling in Italy, but the seasons recommended are from 15th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sept. to 15th Nov., and the months of April and May. The rainy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}winter months should, if possible, be spent in one of the larger{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cities, of which Rome is unquestionably the most interesting. June,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}July, and August are hardly suitable for a tour. The scenery indeed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is then in perfection, and the long days are hailed with satisfaction{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the active traveller; but the fierce rays of an Italian sun seldom{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fail to sap the physical and mental energies. This result is not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occasioned so much by the intensity, as by the protracted duration{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the heat, the sky being frequently cloudless, and not a drop of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rain falling for many weeks in succession. The heat generally mod¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erates about the end of August, when the first showers of autumn{% endraw %}
{% raw %}begin to refresh the parched atmosphere.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Plan. The plan of a tour in Italy must of course be framed in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accordance with the object which the traveller has in view. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Central Italy the principal attraction is Rome itself, and of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other towns described in the present volume the next in importance{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are Siena and Perugia, both of which afford good summer quarters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}owing to their elevated situations. Two other places of great in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}terest are Orvieto and Assist, a short visit to which should not be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}omitted. Arezzo, Cortona, Spoleto, Temi, with its imposing water¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}falls, and Chiusi and Corneto, with their Etruscan antiquities, are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also interesting points, situated near the railway. Volterra, S. Gi-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mignano, Montepulciano, and Viterbo, though less conveniently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situated, are also well worthy of a visit. With regard to the towns{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lying on the coast of the Adriatic, comp. p. 81. Besides these{% endraw %}
{% raw %}points of attraction there are many others in the less frequented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}districts of the interior, which the traveller who desires more than{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a superficial acquaintance with Italy should not fail to explore; and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the farther he diverges from the beaten track, the more he will{% endraw %}
{% raw %}learn of the characteristics of this delightful country.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}III. Language.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The time and labour which the traveller has bestowed on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}study of Italian at home will be amply repaid as he proceeds on his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}journey. It is quite possible for persons entirely ignorant of Italian{% endraw %}

{% raw %}travellers cannot conveniently deviate from the ordinary track, and{% endraw %}
