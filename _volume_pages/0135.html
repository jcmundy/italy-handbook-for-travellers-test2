{% raw %}Orvieto is a small town and episcopal residence , situated on an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}isolated tufa rock, 735 ft. above the Paglia (1165 ft. above the sea-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}level), the Vrbibentum of Procopius, called Urbs Fetus in the 8th{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cent. , whence its name. In the middle ages it was the principal{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stronghold of the Guelphs, and often served the popes as a place{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of refuge.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the E. entrance to the town, which we reach in coming{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the station, and where the old castle mentioned below is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}situated, runs the Corso, the principal street of Orvieto. Two me¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dieval towers rise in this street: opposite the first of these is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Via del Duomo, which leads us straight to the far-famed —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}**Cath,edral (PL 1), a magnificent example of the Italian Gothic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}style, and one of the most interesting buildings in Italy, founded in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}consequence of the 'Miracle of Bolsena' (comp. p. 65). The first stone{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was solemnly laid by Pope Nicholas IV. on 13th Nov. 1290, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}edifice begun under the supervision and according to the plans of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lorenzo Maitani of Siena. The work progressed so rapidly that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1309 Bishop Guido di Farnese was able to read the first mass in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the church. It consists of a nave and aisles, with transept and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rectangular choir. It is lli'/a yds. long and 36 yds. wide, and like{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the cathedrals of Florence and Siena is constructed of alternate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}courses of black and white marble. This cathedral, like those in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other towns, once constituted a great arena for the display of artistic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}skill. The guardians of the building were unwearied in providing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for its ornamentation , and like the curators of modern museums{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who are zealous in their endeavours to secure works by the best{% endraw %}
{% raw %}artists, they did all in their power to obtain the services of the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}masters of the day for the embellishment of their church. — The{% endraw %}
