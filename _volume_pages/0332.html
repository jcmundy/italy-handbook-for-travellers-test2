{% raw %}The Palatine Hill, situated on the S. side of the Forum, rises in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the form of an irregular quadrangle. In ancient times it was bounded{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the N. side, towards the Capitol, by the Velabrum and the Forum{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Boarium (p. 248); on the W., towards the Aventine, by the Circus lla-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ximus (p. 250); on the S., towards the Ctelius, by the Via Triumphalis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the Via Appia (now Via di S. Gregorio). The hill is 1900 yds. in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}circumference, and the highest point, (S. Bonaventura) is 168 ft. above{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the sea-level, or 114 ft. above the level of ancient. Rome. The Palatine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was the original site and the centre of the embryo mistress of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}world, the Roma Quadrala, fragments of whose walls have been brought{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to light at five different places, thus enabling us to trace the situation{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of these venerable fortifications with tolerable precision. The wall{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appears to have encircled the whole of the hill about half-way{% endraw %}
{% raw %}up its slopes, and to have been penetrated by gates at three places{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only. The situation of two of these, the Porta Mugionis or Mugonia{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PI. 13), and the Porta Romana or Romanula (PL 4), has been as¬{% endraw %}
