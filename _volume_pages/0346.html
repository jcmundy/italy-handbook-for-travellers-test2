{% raw %}On the other side of the transverse street rises the picturesque{% endraw %}
{% raw %}"House of Crescentius (PL II, 18, 3), or Casa di Rienzi, or di Pi-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lato, as it is commonly called, a building constructed of brick with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a singular admixture of antique fragments. On the side, Via del{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ricovero, a long inscription records that 'this lofty house was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected hy Nicholas, son of Crescens, not from motives of ambition,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but as a reminiscence of the ancient glory of Rome'. TheCrescentii{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were the most powerful noble family in Rome at the close of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}10th cent., but the house, the oldest existing specimen of mediseval{% endraw %}
{% raw %}domestic architecture, does not date from an earlier period than the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}11th, or perhaps 12th cent. The building was originally much more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extensive, and was intended to command the bridge over the Tiber.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Ponte Rotto (PL II, 18) crosses from this point to Traste¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vere (p. 329). The ancient bridge which once stood here is supposed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to have been the Pons Aimilius, built in B. C. 181. After frequent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}restorations, the two arches next the left bank fell in 1598, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bridge was never rebuilt; and thence its present name. In 1853{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an iron chain-bridge was thrown across the gap (5 c). The bridge{% endraw %}
{% raw %}affords a picturesque view: on the right the island of the Tiber, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}form resembling a ship; on the left the Aventine; below, the influx{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Cloaca Maxima, and extensive embankments which protect the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}banks against the violence of the current.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}If, in proceeding from the Forum through the Via di S. Teodoro, we{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leave the Janus Quadrifrons (p. 248) on the right, we soon reach, in the Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}de' Fenili, at the corner, the church of S. Anastasia (PL II, 21) mentioned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as early as 499, frequently restored, and finally modernised during{% endraw %}
{% raw %}last century. By the buttresses of the interior the ancient, columns are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still standing. In the left aisle is the monument of Card. Angelo Mai.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Below the church are ancient structures belonging to the Circus Maximus,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and still earlier remains of the walls of Roma Quadrata.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Via de' Cerchi runs between the Palatine and Aventine, where,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as its name suggests, was situated the Circus Maximus, which was origi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nally instituted by the kings, afterwards extended by Ca-sar and furnished{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with stone seats, and lastly more highly decorated by the emperors. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the time of Pliny it was capable of containing 260,000 spectators , and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after subsequent extensions the number of places was increased to 385,000.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The last race which took place here was under the auspices of King Totilas{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 549. at a time when the city was to a great, extent in ruins. In the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}centre ran a spina, or longitudinal wall which connected the inelae, or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}goals, and determined tlie length of the course. With a few trifling ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceptions, the walls of the circus have entirely disappeared; but its form{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is distinctly traceable from a higher point, such as the Palatine. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Jewish burial-ground is situated within the Circus, at. the base of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Aventine.{% endraw %}
