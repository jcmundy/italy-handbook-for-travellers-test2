{% raw %}towards the Piazza di Venezia by P. Amati. The handsome court,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}surrounded by arcades, is entered from the Corso (No. 305). To{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The Doria Gallery resembles all the other Roman galleries in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being devoted to no particular school, and in possessing examples{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of every different style, but on the whole the founders seem to have{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shown a preference for works of the 17th cent. The ante-chambers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are badly lighted, so that the pictures are not seen to advantage,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but most of the works here are hardly above mediocrity. The visi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tor should, however, note the predella of a now lost altar-piece by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Peselli (TI. Room, 23, 29), a good specimen of early Florentine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painting, and the Madonnas of Niccolb Rondinelli (II. Room, 12,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}43), a little known master, who has happily imitated the golden{% endraw %}
{% raw %}colouring of the old Venetians, and is one of Giov. Bellini's ablest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}followers.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The gems of the collection arc hi the three galleries and in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}corner cabinet. Raphael, the prince of ciuquecentists, is represented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the portraits of two Venetian scholars, Andrea Navagero and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Agostino Beazzano (in the corner cabinet); but their authenticity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has been questioned and their touch indeed is somewhat different{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from Raphael's usual style. The vigorous tone and breadth of co¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}louring may, however, have been occasioned by his habit of fresco{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painting. Johanna of Arragon is a copy only (II. Gall. 53), and so{% endraw %}
{% raw %}too is Titian's Periods of Life (II. Gall. 20). Pordenone's Herodias{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(II. Gall. 40), and Lor. Lotto's portrait of himself (II. Gall. 34),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the other hand, are admirable Venetian works. The portrait of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Andrea Doria by Sebastian del Piombo is not Venetian in character,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but is interesting from the faculty displayed by the master of im¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}parting an air of grandeur to a repulsive subject (corner cabinet).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}With this work the visitor should compare the portrait of Pope In¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nocent X., by Velasquez, in the same room. The colouring of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}latter is strikingly rich, completely eclipsing Piombo's massiveness{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of style. The skilful manner in which the three shades of red are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}blended should be particularly noticed.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Garofalo, though not a master of the highest rank, has produced{% endraw %}
