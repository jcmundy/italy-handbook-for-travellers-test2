{% raw %}The Via del Portico di Ottavia , formerly the Pescheria , or fish-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}market, which presents a busy scene on Friday mornings, leads from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Piazza Giudea to the Portico of Octavia. Between the Pescheria{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the Tiber lies the Ghetto (PL II, 17), the quarter allotted by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Paul IV. to the Jews, who in ancient and mediaeval times occupied{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a quarter in Trastevere, formerly closed by a gate. It consists of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}several streets parallel with the river, and connected by narrow{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lanes. The same pope compelled the Jews to wear yellow head¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gear, and pay unusually heavy taxes; and among other oppressive{% endraw %}
{% raw %}exactions, they had to provide the prizes for the horse-races at tlie{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Carnival. The traveller may explore this quarter for the sake of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}observing the marked oriental type of its occupants, and the charac¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}teristic industry with which they seek to counteract the disadvan¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tages of their social position. The Via della Fiumara, the nearest to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the river, leads to the Ponte de' Quattro Capi (see p. 328).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Via del Portico di Ottavia leads in a straight direction to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the interesting remains of the Portico of Octavia (PL II, 17, IS),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which was erected by Augustus on the site of a similar structure of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Metellus (B. C. 149), and dedicated to his sister. Under Titus it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was destroyed by a conflagration which raged in this quarter of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}city, but was restored by Sept. Severus and Caracalla in 203, as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the inscription records. This building was in the form of a colon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nade enclosing an oblong space, within which stood temples of Ju¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}piter Stator and Juno. Columns from this structure are frequently{% endraw %}
{% raw %}seen built into other edifices. It was adorned with many admirable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}works of art which formed part of the Macedonian booty, and it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was here that the Medici Venus was found. In 770 the church of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Angelo in Pescheria was built on the ruins of the colonnade by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Stephen III., but, having been frequently restored, it has entirely{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lost its mediaeval character. In pursuance of a bull issued in 1584,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Jews were formerly compelled to hear sermons here on their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sabbath, in which the Old Testament was explained to them in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accordance with the doctrines of the church.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Beyond the colonnade the Via del Teatro di Marcello next leads{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Theatre of Marcellus (PL II, 17, 5), which was begun by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Caesar, and completed B. C. 13 by Augustus, who named it after his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nephew, the son of Octavia. The twelve arches still standing on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}external wall of the space for the spectators are now occupied by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}smiths and other artizans as workshops. The lower story, partly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}filled up, is in the Doric, the second in the Ionic style, above which,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as in the case of the Colosseum, a third probably rose in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Corinthian order. It is said to have accommodated 20,000 spectators.{% endraw %}
