{% raw %}Wall of Servius. ROME. II. The Hills. 177{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Opposite the entrance of the church of S. Maria degli Angeli,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and through the middle of the circular wall of the Therm*, runs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Via Nazionale, which intersects the Via Quattro Fontane, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is to be continued in a straight direction to the corner of Via del{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Quirinale and Via Magnanapoli (p. 172). Immediately to the right{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the entrance to the Piazza delle Terme rises the War Office. —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At Via Nazionale 354 is the Galleria Tenerani, a complete collection{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the original models of the sculptor P. Tenerani (d. 1869) ; open on{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wed. 1-4 ; on other days a fee must be paid.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the E. side of the Piazza delle Terme is the extensive new{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Railway Station (PL I, 25), constructed by Miriere and Bianchi.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In front of it rises an imposing fountain, fed by the Aqua Marcia,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which has been recently restored.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Within the precincts of the railway-station part of the Wall of Servius,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which protected the city on this undefended side, has been exposed to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}view in consequence of the construction of the railway. The wall, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is supported by a strong embankment, with its broad moat and numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}towers, may be traced as far as the arch of fiallienus.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the right (S.W.) runs the new Via del Viminale, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}farther on intersects the Via delle Quattro Fontane.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Passing the station, and traversing the Piazza delle Terme{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lengthwise, we reach the Porta S. Lorenzo (p. 181) in t/4 hr.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Turning to the left between the station and the Therma, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}passing the new buildings of the quarter which is now springing up{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here, we reach in 10 min. the Campo di Maccao, or Campo Mili¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tate, the camp of the Praetorians of imperial Rome. It was originally{% endraw %}
{% raw %}established by Tiberius, but destroyed by Constantine so far as it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lay without the town-wall, from which it projects in a quadrangular{% endraw %}
{% raw %}form. At the end to the left, and on the side, traces of gates are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still distinguished ; the wall was skirted by a passage, under which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are several small chambers. Since the end of the papal re'gime it has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}again been devoted to military purposes; parades and reviews are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}held here, and the large, newly erected barracks impart unwonted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}life to the bleak ramparts.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}From the Quattro Fontane to S. Maria Maggiore is a walk of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}10 minutes. We first descend the Quirinal, crossing the new Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nazionale (see above), which leads to the Piazza delle Terme. We{% endraw %}
{% raw %}then traverse the Viminal, which is here of insignificant height;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the left diverges the new Via del Viminale (see above) leading to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the station. In the valley between the Viminal and Esquiline, in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the first side-street to the right, is situated —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Pudenziana (PL II, 25; open till 9 a. m.; custodian, Via{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Quattro Fontane 81), traditionally the most ancient church in Rome,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected on the spot where St. Pudens and his daughters Praxedis{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Pudentiana, who entertained St. Peter, are said to have lived.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The church, the earliest record of which dates from 499, has been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}frequently restored, the greatest alterations having been made in{% endraw %}
