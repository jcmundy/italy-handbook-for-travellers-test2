{% raw %}ANCIENT ART.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}group displaying the established forms and traditional models,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which in respect of means of expression and motive are the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}worthy inheritance of Greek art at its best. Yet these sarcophagi,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}regarded even as Roman works, are by no means of early origin. It{% endraw %}
{% raw %}must not, however, be forgotten in estimating the quality of work{% endraw %}
{% raw %}bestowed upon the sarcophagus, that it was not intended to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}closely inspected by the light of day, but would be consigned to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}twilight of the tomb, where a stray gleam of light might but for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a moment reveal its detail. Hence, in the execution of these reliefs{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the object was to give prominence to leading features, without an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}overscrupulous nicety of finish, and this end has been attained{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with a success worthy of all admiration. It has been ascertained{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beyond doubt, that the introduction of the sarcophagus as described{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above was coeval with a mode of burial which became the fashion{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in Imperial times ; otherwise the artistic merits of these monuments{% endraw %}
{% raw %}might well have misled us in computing their age. The great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}majority of Roman sarcophagi belong to the 2nd and 3rd centuries{% endraw %}
{% raw %}after Christ, and to an even later period.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Early Christian Sarcophagi simply repeat and perpetuate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}preceding pagan models. It is a peculiar coincidence that these in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conspicuous memorials should have contributed to the revival of art{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the middle ages. Niccolo Pisano found a fertile source of inspi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ration in the Roman sarcophagi of the Campo Santo in Pisa; nor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}did Peruzzi and Raphael disdain to use them as models.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}With this passing glance at the homage thus done by Raphael and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his compeers to the art of antiquity, these pages may fitly conclude.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The endeavour has not been to fetter the judgment of the reader,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}but rather so to direct his observation and stimulate his interest as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to give him that self-reliance which alone will arouse in him an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}intelligent interest, and afford him a genuine pleasure in what{% endraw %}
{% raw %}he sees. To praise the creations of great artists in empty or mere{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conventional phrase would simply offend. They alone will exper¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ience the full measure of delight to be derived from the contem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plation of these treasures, who rely upon their own judgment and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cultivate to the utmost the delicacy of their perceptions.{% endraw %}
