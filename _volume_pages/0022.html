{% raw %}IV. Passports. Custom-house. Luggage.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Passports, though not required in Italy, are occasionally useful.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Registered letters, for example, will not be delivered to strangers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unless they exhibit a passport to prove their identity. In the remote{% endraw %}
{% raw %}districts, too, where the public safety demands a more rigorous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}supervision, the traveller is sometimes asked for his credentials.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Italian police authorities are generally civil and obliging.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Custom-House. The examination of luggage at the Italian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}custom-houses is usually lenient. Tobacco and cigars are the articles{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chiefly sought for. At the gates of most of the Italian towns a tax{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(dazio consumo) is levied on comestibles, but travellers' luggage is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}passed at the barriers (limite daziario) on a simple declaration that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it contains no such articles.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Luggage. If possible, luggage should never be sent to Italy by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}goods-train, as it is liable to damage, pilferage, and undue custom¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}house detention. If the traveller is obliged to forward it in this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}way, he should employ a trustworthy agent at the frontier and send{% endraw %}
{% raw %}him the keys. As a rule it is advisable, and often in the end less{% endraw %}
{% raw %}expensive, never to part from one's luggage, and to superintend the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}custom-house examination in person.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}A few words on the pronunciation may be acceptable to persons unac¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}quainted with the language. C before e and i is pronounced like the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}English ch; g before e and i like j. Before other vowels c and g are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hard. Ch and gh, which generally precede e or i, are hard. Sc before e{% endraw %}
{% raw %}or i is pronounced like sh; gn and gl between yowels like nyi and lyi.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The vowels a, e, i, o, u are pronounced ah, a, ee, p, oo. — In ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dressing persons of the educated classes 'Ella' or 'Lei', with the 3rd pers.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sing., should always be employed (addressing several at once, 'loro' with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 3rd pers. pi.). 'Voi' is used in addressing waiters,_ drivers, etc., 'tu' by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}those only who are proficient in the language. 'Voi' is the usual mode of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}address among the Neapolitans, but is generally regarded as inelegant or{% endraw %}
{% raw %}uncourteous.{% endraw %}
