{% raw %}252 IV. Ancient Rome. ROME. Pyramid of Cestius.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Amongst many illustrious names the eye will fall with interest upon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that of the poet Shelley (d. 1822), 'cor cordium', whose heart only was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}buried here (near the upper, or Eastern, wall). His remains were burned{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the bay of Spc/ia, where they were washed on shore. The tombstone{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of John Reals, who also rests here, bears the melancholy- inscription, 'Here{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lies one whose name was writ in water'.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Pyramid of Cestius (PL III, 16), originally situated in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Via Ostiensis, was enclosed by Aurelian within the city-wall.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}This is the tomb of Caius Cestius, who died within the last thirty-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}years before Christ. The Egyptian pyramidal form was not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unfrequently adopted by the Romans in the construction of their{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tombs. That of Cestius is constructed of brick and covered with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marble blocks; height 116 ft., width of each side of the base 98 ft.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}According to the principal Inscription on the E. and W. sides (-C.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Cestius L. F. Pob. Epulo. Pr. Tr. PI. VII. vir Epulonum'), the deceased{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was praetor, tribune of the people, and member of the college of Septem-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}viri Epulonum, or priests whose office was to conduct the solemn sacri¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ficial banquets. The inscription on the W. side below records that the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monument, was erected in 330 days under the supervision of L. Pon¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tius Mela and the freedman Pothus. Alexander VII. caused the some¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}what deeply imbedded monument to be extricated in 1(303, on which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occasion, besides the two cotumns of white marble, the colossal bronze{% endraw %}
{% raw %}foot, now in the collection of bronzes in the Capitoline Museum (p. 216),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was found. According to the inscription on the pedestal, it appears to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have belonged to a colossal statue of Cestius. — The Vault (19 ft. long,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}13 ft. wide, and 16 ft. high) was originally accessible by ladders only.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The present entrance was made by order of Alexander VII. (key kept, by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the custodian of the Protestant cemetery). The vaulting shows traces{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of painting.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Crossing the meadows, we next proceed to *Monte Testaccio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(PL III, 13), an isolated mound, 164ft. in height, rising not far{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from the Tiber, which, as the name indicates, consists entirely of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}broken pottery. When and how this hill was formed is still an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}unsolved mystery. The popular belief was that the vessels in which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}conquered nations paid their tribute-money were broken here, while{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the learned have assumed that potteries once existed in the vicinity,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and that the broken fragments together with other rubbish were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}collected here to be used for building purposes. Others have con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nected this remarkable hill with the Neronian conflagration, or with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the magazines situated on the Tiber near the old harbour (p. 250).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The hill existed prior to the Aurelian wall, and brick stamps found{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there date from the first centuries of the Christian era. It is now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perforated on all sides by cellars , in some of which wine is sold,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and on holidays it is much visited by pleasure-seekers (pp. 107,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}116). — The summit is marked by a wooden cross and commands{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a magnificent **Panorama: —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the N., the city, beyond it the mountains surrounding the crater of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Baceano, then the isolated Soracte with its five peaks. To the E. the Sabine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mts., in the background the imposing Leonessa, in the nearer chain M. Gen-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}naro, at its base Monticelli, farther to the right Tivoli. Beyond this chain{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the summits of M. Velino above the Lago Fucino are visible. To the S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Tivoli appears Palestrina. After a depression, above which some of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Volsci.-in Mis. rise, follow the Alban Mts.: on tin- buttress farthest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}E. is Colonna, beyond it Frascati, higher up Rocca di Papa, M. Cavo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with its monastery, below it Marino, finally to the right Castel Uandolfo.{% endraw %}
