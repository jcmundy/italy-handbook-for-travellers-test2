{% raw %}Brancaleoni demolished 140 of these strongholds, the majority of{% endraw %}

{% raw %}mediaeval architecture is to be seen in the Casa di Pilato or di{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rienzo. Built by one Nicolao, son of Cresoentius (probably in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}12th century), its chief ornamentation consists of marble fragments{% endraw %}
{% raw %}apparently picked up at random and put together in the wildest{% endraw %}
{% raw %}confusion.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the close of the 12th century brighter days dawned for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman art. 'Magister Romanus' now became a title which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}artist was proud to append to his surname. A speciality in decora¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tive art appeared in Rome about this time which did not connect{% endraw %}
{% raw %}itself, it is true, with the traditions of antique art, though ready{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to utilise its material, without, however, resort to the depredations{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of a bygone age. And material was still at hand in richest abun¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dance, in an endless array of shattered marbles. These were divided{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and subdivided, out or sawn into minute slabs, arranged in patterns,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enlivened by the introduction of stained glass and gold leaf, pre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}senting as a whole a richly coloured decorative effect. These marble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosaics adorn the flooring of churches, altar sides, episcopal chairs,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pulpits, and doorways; they enliven monumental sculpture, they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}fill the flutings of the elegantly twisted columns which bore the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Easter candles or adorn the entablature of cloistered courts. This{% endraw %}
{% raw %}art became the monopoly of particular families and was regularly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}transmitted from generation to generation. The monumental{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marbles of this time are generally known as Cosmato Work, a name{% endraw %}
{% raw %}derived from two members of a family thus privileged. Such work{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is frequently to be met with in Rome. Conspicuous among the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mosaic floorings are those of S. Maria Maggiore, S. Maria in Tras¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tevere, and S. Lorenzo Fuori le Mura (12th century). S. Ciemente{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and S. Giorgio possess altar tabernacles of Cosmato work and 5.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Lorenzo the finest example in its pulpit. Of similar work in cloisters{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(S. Sabina,Lateran) the best specimen is in the convent of S. Paolo{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(13th century). Cosmato work is not infrequently found elsewhere{% endraw %}
{% raw %}than in Rome. It is uncertain how far this Roman work is connected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with kindred examples to be met with in Southern Italy. In tech¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}nical detail some differences are to be detected, such as the more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}copious use of the glass pastes by the artists of the South. On the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other hand we fancy that the identity of pattern in the mosaics of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}theCappella Palatina in Palermo with those of S. Lorenzo cannot be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}accidental.{% endraw %}
