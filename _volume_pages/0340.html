{% raw %}Adjoining the atrium are three chambers, the most S. of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the Lararium (PI. 16), or chapel of the Lares or household-gods.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On a pedestal at the extremity of the chapel is a small square altar{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in marble with figures of the Genius Familiaris and the Lares. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}former stands in front with covered head ; the latter are represented{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the sides in the typical style common in Pompeian works of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}kind, with boots, a short chiton, a rhyton or drinking-horn in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}raised hand, and a situla or pitcher in the other.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The second apartment is the Tablinum (PI. 10), already men¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tioned, which in private dwellings was the principal sitting-room.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}It was here used as an Aula Regia, or throne-room, where the em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perors granted audiences. This extensive hall, 39 yds. hy 49 yds.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with its large semicircular apse which was occupied by the throne,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and its eight niches alternately round and square, containing the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still existing pedestals, was originally entirely covered; hut an ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}equate idea of its magnificence can hardly now be formed, as it has{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been deprived of its decorated ceiling, while the walls have lost{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their marble covering, the niches their statues, and the pedestals{% endraw %}
{% raw %}their colossal figures.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The third apartment is the Basilica (PI. 17), where the emperor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pronounced his judicial decisions. The semicircular tribune was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}separated from the space allotted to the litigants by a marble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}screen, a fragment of which still stands here. This space was flank¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed on each side by a narrow colonnade , some of the bases of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and one column are preserved.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the W. of the tablinum is situated the Peristylium (PI. 18),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two-thirds of which only have been excavated (one-third on the S.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side is covered by the court of the adjoining Salesian nunnery),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a large rectangular garden, 58 yds. in length, originally surround¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ed by a colonnade. Its imposing dimensions and a few traces of its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}marble covering (giallo antico) are now the sole indications of its{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ancient magnificence. The open space in the centre was doubtless{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occupied by fountains, trees, and flowers.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the N.W. corner steps descend to two subterranean chambers con{% endraw %}
{% raw %}taining traces of stucco decorations and painting. These belonged to a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}private house of the republican period, over which the palaces of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Flavii were erected.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Opening on the peristyle along its entire width was the Tricli¬{% endraw %}

{% raw %}could enjoy a view of the fountains and trees in the garden. In{% endraw %}
