{% raw %}A characteristic of this period is to be found in the numerous,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}many-storied towers of red brick which contrast so strongly with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the monuments of ancient Rome. This style of architecture was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}developed in the Carlovingian epoch, although most of these towers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}now extant were not erected before the 12th or 13th century. In{% endraw %}
{% raw %}still greater numbers sprang up towers of a defensive character, a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}few only of which, such as the so-called Torre di Nerone (p. 172),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are still preserved. The forest of towers , belonging to numerous{% endraw %}
{% raw %}different owners, which reared themselves over the ruins of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mistress of the world, affords at the same time a clue to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}character of the whole epoch; for, in spite of the nominal sway{% endraw %}
{% raw %}exercised over the greater part of Europe by the pope and the em¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}peror, continual feuds raged both at Rome and elsewhere between{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the temporal and spiritual powers, and between the nobility and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the populace. The great monuments of antiquity were now doomed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to utter destruction, and their fate is thus described by the historian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gregovorius (iii, 565): — 'Charlemagne had already set the ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ample of carrying off ancient columns and sculptures to adorn his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cathedral at Aix-la-Chapelle, and the popes, who regarded the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}greatest monuments of Rome as the property of the state, possessed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}neither taste, nor time , nor ability to take measures for their pre¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}servation. The plundering of ancient buildings became the order{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the day. The priests were indefatigable in transferring antique{% endraw %}
{% raw %}columns and marbles to their churches ; the nobles, and even the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abbots , took possession of magnificent ancient edifices which they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}disfigured by the addition of modern towers ; and the citizens estab¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lished their workshops, rope-walks, and smithies in the towers{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and circuses of imperial Rome. The fisherman selling his fish near{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the bridges over the Tiber, the butcher displaying his meat at the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}theatre of Marcellus, and the baker exposing his bread for sale, de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}posited their wares on the magnificent slabs of marble which had{% endraw %}
{% raw %}once been used as seats by the senators in the theatre or circus and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}perhaps by Caesar, Mark Antony, Augustus, and other masters of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the world. The elaborately sculptured sarcophagi of Roman heroes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}were scattered in every direction and converted into cisterns, wash¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing-vats, and troughs for swine; and the table of the tailor and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shoemaker was perhaps formed of the cippus of some illustrious{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Roman, or of a slab of alabaster once used by some noble Roman{% endraw %}
{% raw %}matron for the display of her jewellery. For several centuries Rome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may be said to have resembled a vast lime-kiln, into which the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}costliest marbles were recklessly cast for the purpose of burning{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lime ; and thus did the Romans incessantly pillage, burn, dismantle,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and utterly destroy their glorious old city'.{% endraw %}
