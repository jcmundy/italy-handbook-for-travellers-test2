{% raw %}HOTELS.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}xxi{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Walking Tours. An Italian rarely walks if he can possibly drive;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and how walking can afford pleasure is to him an inexplicable mys¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tery. The remark has frequently been made to the Editor: '■Lei e{% endraw %}
{% raw %}signore e va a piedi ?!' In the more frequented districts, however,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}such as the vicinity of Rome, the natives are accustomed to this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mania of foreigners, and are no longer surprised to find them ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ploring the Campagna and the Sabine and Alban Mts. on foot.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}There seems, however, to be a growing taste for walking among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Italians themselves, as a great many stations of the Italian{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Club Alpino have recently been established for the purpose of ren¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dering the Apennines more accessible to travellers. Cool and clear{% endraw %}
{% raw %}weather should if possible be selected, and the scirocco carefully{% endraw %}
{% raw %}avoided. The height of summer is of course unsuitable for tours of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}this kind.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Biding. A horse (cavallo) or donkey (sommaro'), between which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the difference of expense is slight, will often be found serviceable.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}especially in mountainous districts. The attendant (pedone) acts as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a guide and servant for the time being. Riding is now common{% endraw %}
{% raw %}among the Alban and Sabine Mts. , where animals are provided for{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the use of ladies also. A previous bargain should be made, tutto{% endraw %}
{% raw %}compreso, a gratuity being added if the traveller is satisfied.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}VIII. Hotels.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}First Class Hotels , comfortably fitted up , are to be found at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rome, Siena, Perugia, and at most of the principal resorts of tra¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}vellers in Central Italy. Several of those at Rome are kept by Swiss{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and German landlords. Room 2!/2-5fr., bougie 75c. to 1 fr., atten¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dance 1 fr., table d'hdte 5 fr., and so on. Families, for whose re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ception the hotels are often specially fitted up , should make an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}agreement with the landlord with regard to pension (8-15 fr. each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}person per day). Visitors are expected to dine at the table d'hote ;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}otherwise the charge for rooms is raised , or the inmate is informed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}that they are required for some one else. The cuisine is a mixture{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of French and Italian.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Second Class Hotels are thoroughly Italian in their ar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rangements , and they are rarely very clean or comfortable. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}charges are little more than one-half of the above. There is no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}table d'hote, but there is generally a trattoria connected with the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}house , where refreshments a la carte or a dinner a prezzo fisso{% endraw %}
{% raw %}may be procured at any hour. These inns will often be found con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}venient and economical by the voyageur en garcon, and the better{% endraw %}
