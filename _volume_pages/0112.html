{% raw %}40 Route 0. AREZZO. From Florence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Arezzo was the birthplace of many distinguished men, of whom may{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be mentioned: C. Cilnius Maecenas (d. 9 A.IX), the friend of Augustus{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and patron of Virgil and Horace; the Benedictine monk Guido Aretino{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1000-1050), the inventor of our present system of musical notation;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Francesco Petrarca, the greatest lvric poet of Italy, born of Florentine{% endraw %}
{% raw %}parents in 1304 (d. 1374); Pietro Aretino, the satirist (1492-1557); several{% endraw %}
{% raw %}members of the noble family of the Accolti, jurists and historians, in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1517th cent.; A. Cesalpini, "the botanist and physician (1519-1603); Franc.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Redi, the physician and humourist (d. 169s). — Arezzo has also produced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}several artists; Margaritone (about 1236), a painter and sculptor of no{% endraw %}
{% raw %}great importance; Spinello Aretino (1318-1410), an able pupil of Giotto,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}whose style he steadily followed and rendered popular (his best works{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are in S. Miniato near Florence, in the Campo Santo at Pisa, and in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Palazzo Comunale in Siena); at a later period Giorgio Vasari (1512-74),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the painter, architect, and biographer of artists. The town, however,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}never possessed a school of its own. Its requirements in the province{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of art, which were at their height in the 13-14th cent., were fulfilled by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Florentine and Sienese masters, and Giotto, Lippo Memmi, Lorenzelti,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and others were employed here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Leaving the station, we follow the new Via Guido Monaco lead¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing in 5 min. to the Via Cavour. Here, to the right, in the small{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Piazza S. Francesco, is a Monument to Count Fossombrone (b. in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Arezzo 1754, d. 1844; PI. 1; p. 42), and the church of—{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Francesco (PI. 2), remarkable for its frescoes, dating from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the 15th cent.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the Choir : 'Frescoes by Piero della Francesco, the master of Luca{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Signorelli (best light in the evening). They narrate the legend of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Holy Cross, according to which a seed of the tree of knowledge, planted{% endraw %}
{% raw %}upon Adam's grave, grew up to be a tree. Solomon caused the tree to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}felled and abridge to be constructed of the wood, of which the Queen of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Sheba afterwards discovered the origin. At a later period it was used{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for making the Holy Cross. The Emp. Heraclius rescued the cross in a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}battle with the Persians, and it was afterwards re-discovered by St. He¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lena. All these scenes, from the death of Adam down to the finding of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cross, are pourtrayed by Piero with great technical skill, in which respect,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}as well as in his appreciation of the nude, he surpassed all his contem¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}poraries. His pictures, however, are stiff and destitute of gracefulness.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— The Evangelists on the ceiling have been attributed to Bicci di Lo¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}renzo. — The Nave, recently freed from whitewash, contains frescoes by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Spinello Aretino, sadly injured. The following pictures are also placed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}here temporarily; Madonna surrounded by numerous saints, a characteristic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}work of the Sienese master Pietro Lorenzelti, brought from the church{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Pieve (sec below); and St. Rochus being invoked during the plague,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}two pictures by Bartolommeo della Gatla, a master who was influenced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Signorelli.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Via Cavour forms a right angle with the Corso Vittorio{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Emanuele, the principal street of the town. Ascending this street,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}we observe on the right the interesting church of —{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. Maria della Pieve (PI. 3), which is said to have been built{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the beginning of the 9th cent, on the site of a temple of Bacchus;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tower and facade of 1216. The latter is very peculiar, consisting{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of four series of columns, distributed with singular incongruity. An¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cient sculptures over the doors. The Interior, now undergoing resto¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ration, consists of a nave and aisles with a dome.{% endraw %}
