{% raw %}'Thou art the garden of the world, the home{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Of all Art yields, and Nature can decree;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}E'en in thy desert, what is like to thee?{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thy very weeds are beautiful, thy waste{% endraw %}
{% raw %}More rich than other climes' fertility,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thy wreck a glory, and thy ruin graced{% endraw %}
{% raw %}With an immaculate charm which cannot be defaced.'{% endraw %}

{% raw %}I. Travelling Expenses. Money.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Expenses. The cost of a tour in Italy depends of course on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}traveller's resources and habits, but, as already stated in the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}part of this Handbook, it need not exceed that incurred in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}more frequented parts of the continent. The average expenditure of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a single traveller may be estimated at 25 francs per day, or at 12-15{% endraw %}
{% raw %}francs when a prolonged stay is made at one place ; but persons{% endraw %}
{% raw %}acquainted with the language and habits of the country may easily{% endraw %}
{% raw %}restrict their expenses to still narrower limits. Those who travel as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}members of a party effect a considerable saving by sharing the ex¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}pense of guides, carriages, and other items. When ladies are of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}party, the expenses are generally greater.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Money. The French monetary system is now in use throughout{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the whole of Italy. The franc (lira or franco~) contains 100 centesimi;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1 fr. 25c. = Is. = 1 German mark = 50 Austrian kreuzers. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}precious metals are rarely seen in Italy. In copper (bronzo or rame){% endraw %}
{% raw %}there are coins of 1, 2, 5, and 10 centesimi. A piece of 5 c. is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}called a soldo, or sou, and as the lower classes often keep their ac¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}counts in soldi, the traveller will find it useful to accustom himself{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to this mode of reckoning. See also the Money Table opposite the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}title-page.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Banknotes. Since the introduction of a paper currency during{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the war of 1866, at a compulsory rate of exchange , gold and silver{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have entirely disappeared from ordinary circulation, and bundles of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}small notes have taken their place. For these the purses used in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most other countries are quite unsuitable, but one adapted for the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}purpose may be bought in Italy for 172-2 fr.; in addition to which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a strong pouch for copper will be found useful. The endless{% endraw %}
